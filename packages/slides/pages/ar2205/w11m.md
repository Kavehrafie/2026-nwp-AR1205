<!-- 

Chuck Close's Big Self-Portrait (1967-1968) is a landmark painting that launched his career and redefined contemporary portraiture. This monumental, photorealistic work was a deliberate departure from the abstract art popular at the time .

Here is a summary of its key aspects:

🎨 Creation and Technique: Close created the painting using a groundbreaking method. He took a black-and-white photograph of himself, overlaid it with a grid, and meticulously reproduced the image square by square onto a massive canvas . The final piece, measuring over 9 feet (2.7 meters) tall, was painted using thinned black acrylic paint applied with non-traditional tools like an airbrush and even an eraser on a power drill to achieve a slick, photographic surface .

🖼️ Description of the Image: The painting is a stark, larger-than-life close-up of the artist's head and bare shoulders. He stares intently at the viewer through thick-rimmed glasses, his hair unkempt, his face unshaven, and a cigarette dangling from the corner of his mouth .

💡 Artistic Significance: Big Self-Portrait was Close's first self-portrait and his first major success . It established the portrait as his central theme and introduced his signature technique of using photography and grids. The work challenges viewers by forcing them to reconcile the realistic whole image with the abstract, grid-by-grid construction of its surface . Its immense scale was intended to create a "Gulliver's Travels experience," making the face feel like a landscape to be traversed . The painting is now a celebrated piece in the collection of the Walker Art Center in Minneapolis .

The Heart Is Not a Metaphor is the first large-scale survey of Robert Gober’s career to take place in the United States. Gober (American, b. 1954) rose to prominence in the mid-1980s and was quickly acknowledged as one of the most significant artists of his generation. Early in his career he made deceptively simple sculptures of everyday objects—beginning with sinks before moving on to domestic furniture such as playpens, beds, and doors. In the 1990s, his practice evolved from single works to theatrical room-sized environments. Featuring loans from institutions and private collections in North America and Europe, along with selections from the artist’s collection, the exhibition includes around 130 works across several mediums, including individual sculptures and immersive sculptural environments and a distinctive body of drawings, prints, and photographs. - One of the most influential sculptors of his generation, Charles Ray (American, b. 1953) has been making work for more than four decades. His sculptures are often unsettling in their hyperrealism, and they explore themes of identity, gender, and the human body. Ray’s work has been exhibited widely in museums and galleries around the world, and he has received numerous awards and honors for his contributions to contemporary art.


Having been employed as a department-store janitor during his freshman year of college, Charles Ray understands the unease a mannequin can inspire—an inanimate object that one might readily mistake for a live body. His work is also charged with the purely sculptural tensions that exist between surface and interior, armature and appendage, and or size and scale. With Boy, Ray created a particularly disquieting figure. The sculpture stands just shy of six feet tall, the artist’s exact height, yet maintains the softness of youth in its rounded cheeks and limbs.
