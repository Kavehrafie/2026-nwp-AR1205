## connection between the film and the relational aesthetics movement
So Why Does Bourriaud Still Feel Relevant?
Even if Bourriaud wasn't the director's specific model, your comparison remains analytically valuable for two reasons:

Satirizing a "Type": The protagonist is meant to represent a certain archetype prevalent in the contemporary art world—the socially-engaged, jargon-speaking curator whose ideals clash with his behavior. Bourriaud, as the theorist who gave intellectual legitimacy to socially-engaged "relational" art, became emblematic of this archetype.

Thematic Resonance: The film's central installation, The Square, with its mission to be "a sanctuary of trust and caring," directly parodies the kind of relational aesthetics that Bourriaud championed. Christian's failure to live up to the artwork's ideals mirrors a broader critique of how progressive curatorial rhetoric can sometimes ring hollow.

In my earlier note I mentioned the “grotesque paradox” where the curator overshadows the art. The Square literalizes that: Christian becomes the central character, while the art itself is often peripheral. The film’s most famous scene—the performance artist imitating an ape at the gala—shows the curator losing control of the very relational frameworks he claims to orchestrate.

Bourriaud’s influence peaked in the 1990s–2000s with exhibitions like Traffic and his work at the Palais de Tokyo, which championed a generation of artists (Rirkrit Tiravanija, Philippe Parreno, Dominique Gonzalez‑Foerster) who made social situations their medium. By the time The Square was released, that model had become the default language of contemporary museums—often reduced to a hollow institutional brand. Östlund’s film can be read as a critique of how relational aesthetics, once radical, became a form of managerial class performativity.

### The Weather Project 

Danish-Icelandic artist Olafur Eliasson designed his impressively ambitious installation artwork The Weather Project, 2003 for Tate Modern’s Turbine Hall, replicating the effect of a huge sun emerging through a fine mist. Low-frequency lamps around his artificial sun allowed only the golden glow of the sun to dominate the space, reducing all surrounding colors to the magical shades of gold and black. Master of illusion, Eliasson made his glowing orb from a semi-circle of light which is reflected by mirrored panels on the ceiling that complete the circle, lending the upper half of the sun a hazy, shimmering quality that mimics the real sun. These mirrored panels continued across the entire ceiling, allowing visitors to see themselves reflected as if floating in the sky above them, creating the sensation of hovering weightlessly in space.
