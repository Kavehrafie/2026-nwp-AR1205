Fraser identifies five main subfields:

The Art-Market Subfield: (For-profit sector) Art functions as value, determined by supply, demand, and connoisseurship, often aligning with luxury goods.

The Exhibition Subfield: (Public/nonprofit museums, biennials) Art functions as experience (aesthetic, social, or spectacular), shaped by curatorial discourse and competition for patronage.

The Academic Subfield: (Universities, research institutions) Art functions as knowledge and research, framed by critical theory and academic methodologies.

#### Historical Context and Consolidation
Fraser traces how the field expanded from the mid-1990s onward through three waves: the growth of museums and curatorial discourse, the financialization of art for the ultra-wealthy, and the proliferation of academic degree programs. This expansion was enabled by neoliberal globalization and, ironically, by the avant-garde’s historical negation of boundaries. While earlier eras featured contestation over the same institutions, the consolidation of these subfields has created a conservative equilibrium. Instead of struggling over values, the subfields now exist in a state of mutual dependency (ranging from mutualistic to parasitic), which holds the fragmented field together.

Fraser notes that the field’s incorporative power is so vast that anything it touches—including cultural activism or outsider practices—risks being transformed into capital (knowledge, experience, or value) within its economies. She argues that those seeking to exist "outside" the art field are often already defined by their desire to function within it.
