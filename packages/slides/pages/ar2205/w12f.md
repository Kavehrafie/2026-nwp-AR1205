### Christo and Jeanne-Claude, *Wrapped Reichstag*

- **The Work:** *Wrapped Reichstag* (1971–95) involved wrapping the German parliament building in Berlin with **100,000 square meters of fireproof, silver polypropylene fabric**, secured by 15,600 meters of blue rope and a steel substructure .
- **The Journey:** The project was first conceived in **1971**, but it took **24 years** of relentless lobbying to secure approval . The German Bundestag finally gave its blessing by a narrow vote of 292 to 223 on **February 25, 1994** .
- **The Impact:** Installed in **June 1995**, the wrapped building was on view for two weeks and attracted about **5 million visitors**, transforming the historic structure into a powerful symbol of a reunited, democratic Germany .

### When They Started Collaborating

Christo and Jeanne-Claude began their artistic collaboration in **1961**. Their first joint project was *Stacked Oil Barrels* and *Dockside Packages* at the port of Cologne, Germany .

> **Note:** Although they began working together in 1961, they officially published their projects under the name "Christo and Jeanne-Claude" only starting in **1994**. Before that, works were credited solely to "Christo" .

### about the project
Prompted by a suggestion by a Berlin-based journalist, the artists Christo and Jeanne-Claude first conceived the project in 1971. Situated during the Cold War in the heart of the divided city of Berlin, the Reichstag was then under the jurisdiction of both East and West Berlin authorities. It was scarcely used, but such was the tension between the two governments, and the political sensitivity of the site, that the project to wrap the building was rejected three times. It was only following the reunification of Germany and before the reinstatement of the Reichstag as the seat of the national parliament that the project was accepted, nearly a quarter of a century after it had been first proposed.

### Guerrilla Girls (founded 1985, New York)

**Who They Are:** An anonymous collective of feminist activist artists who wear **gorilla masks** to maintain anonymity—keeping the focus on the issues, not individual identities. Members adopt the names of deceased women artists (e.g., Käthe Kollwitz, Frida Kahlo) as pseudonyms.

**Key Work:** *Do Women Have to Be Naked to Get Into the Met. Museum?* (1989)—a iconic poster stating that fewer than 5% of artists in the Modern Art section were women, while 85% of the nudes were female. The work combined hard-hitting statistics with bold, irreverent graphic design.

**Methods:**
- **Public interventions:** Posters, billboards, and stickers placed throughout New York's art district and beyond
- **Guerrilla tactics:** Fact-based critiques of sexism, racism, and institutional exclusion in museums, galleries, and the art market
- **Anonymity:** Allowed them to bypass ego-driven art world hierarchies and protect their careers from backlash

**Significance:** They exemplify collaborative art as **activist practice**—using collective authorship to amplify marginalized voices, challenge institutional power, and democratize who gets to speak on behalf of art.

> **Connection to your theme:** Like Hirschhorn's *Bataille Monument* and Christo/Jeanne-Claude, Guerrilla Girls reject the singular "genius" model of authorship, instead using collaboration to engage public space and intervene in social/political systems.

### Santiago Sierra, *250 cm* (1999)

**The Work:** For the Havana Biennial, Sierra paid **six young Cuban men** the equivalent of **$30 USD** to have a **continuous 250 cm line tattooed across their backs**—one section of the line on each participant.

### Participatory Art ✏️, Relational Aesthetics ✏️

<!-- 

Participatory art is a term that describes a form of art that directly engages the audience in the creative process so that they become participants in the event

### Participatory Art & Relational Aesthetics

**Participatory Art**
- Art that involves **audience engagement** as a core component—viewers become participants, co-creators, or collaborators
- Shifts focus from **object** to **encounter**, from **spectatorship** to **experience**
- Examples: Hirschhorn's *Bataille Monument* (taxi service, restaurant), Sierra's *250 cm* (paid participants)

**Relational Aesthetics**
- Term coined by curator **Nicolas Bourriaud** in the 1990s
- Art as **social interaction**: the artwork is the human relations it creates
- "The artist becomes a **catalyst** rather than a maker of objects"
- Key idea: Art's role is to propose models of sociability within a fragmented, commodified world

**Connection to Your Presentation**
Both frameworks challenge the **singular artist-genius** model, emphasizing:
- Collaboration
- Public space
- Direct engagement with audiences and communities

> *"The artwork is no longer an object to be contemplated, but a moment to be lived."*

**The Name:** *Oda* means "room" in Turkish. The collective began by renting a small apartment in Tarlabaşı, using it as a studio, gathering space, and site for projects—blurring the line between private life and public practice.

**Method:**
- **Site-specific and relational:** Work emerges from direct engagement with neighbors, street life, and the urban fabric
- **Process over product:** Projects unfold over months or years, shaped by ongoing relationships
- **Horizontal collaboration:** No hierarchy—artists, neighbors, children, shopkeepers all become co-participants
- **Anti-gentrification:** The collective resists the role of artists as agents of urban renewal; instead, they work *with* residents facing displacement


### Key Projects

| Project | Year | Description |
|---------|------|-------------|
| **ADA (Gecekondu Project)** | 2005 | Built a traditional *gecekondu* (overnight house—the informal housing structure that defines Istanbul's migrant neighborhoods) inside the Istanbul Biennial. Constructed by migrant workers who had built such houses since the 1970s. A documentation of their labor and a critique of informal housing policies |
| **101.7 EFEM (Radio Project)** | 2007 | A community radio station broadcast from their apartment space during its final days. Neighbors brought portable radios; the radio became a meeting point. The project reflected on displacement as their own space was about to be demolished |


### Philosophy

> *"Everyday life dominates. Instead of avoiding it, Oda Projesi wants to read and evaluate the city through spontaneous formations."*

The collective sees art not as producing objects for galleries, but as **activating relationships**—creating temporary spaces for dialogue, play, and collective action in neighborhoods marked by poverty, migration, and state violence.
