<!-- 
The Second Empire was the period of Napoleon III’s rule in France from 1852 to 1870. He usurped power from the French Second Republic and declared himself Emperor, establishing a regime that was characterized by authoritarian rule, economic modernization, and a focus on urban development.

During this time, Paris underwent a significant transformation under the direction of Baron Haussmann, who was appointed by Napoleon III to oversee the renovation of the city. Haussmann’s plans included the creation of wide boulevards, public parks, and modern infrastructure, which aimed to improve the city’s aesthetics, sanitation, and traffic flow. The transformation of Paris during the Second Empire had a profound impact on the city’s architecture and urban planning, and it remains a defining feature of Parisian identity today.

<!-- 

In 1588, Paris was still largely medieval. The real heart of the city was the Île de la Cité, not the Louvre or the Right Bank. Power, religion, justice, and trade were concentrated there. **Notre-Dame dominated the skyline**, and most Parisians lived close to their work because daily travel was slow and difficult. The city was dense, loud, and often chaotic.

The Seine was central to everything. It was Paris’s main transportation route. Boats carried food, wine, firewood, building stone, and goods from all over France. Streets were muddy, narrow, and poorly organized, so moving heavy cargo by land was inefficient. The river solved that problem.

One important detail often missed: bridges were not just crossings. Many were lined with houses, shops, and workshops. This made them crowded and dangerous. Fires were common, and several bridges collapsed over time.

That’s why the Pont Neuf, whose construction began in 1578, was revolutionary. It was the first Paris bridge without houses, wide enough for carts and pedestrians, and equipped with sidewalks. When it opened a few years later, it completely changed how people moved across the city. Today, it’s the oldest bridge in Paris still standing.

Fun fact: in the 1500s, Paris had no street numbers. People gave directions using shop signs, fountains, or churches. Getting lost was normal — even for locals.

The painting shown here was created by Théodor Josef Hubert Hoffbauer, an artist known for his detailed reconstructions of old Paris. It is now kept at the Musée Carnavalet, the museum dedicated to the history of the city.

### Biography (Architect)

- **Architect:** Charles Garnier (1825–1898).
- **Commission:** Selected through a competition in 1861 for a new opera house commissioned by Napoleon III.
- **Style:** The building is the epitome of **Second Empire style**—opulent, eclectic, and monumental.
- **Significance:** Garnier was relatively young and unknown at the time of the commission. The Opéra became his masterpiece and defined his legacy.


### Formal Description

- **Exterior:**
    - Copper dome (now green with patina) is actually a **false dome**; a second dome exists inside.
    - The space between the domes houses the mechanism for retracting the seven-ton chandelier.
    - A raised area behind the dome contains pulleys for stage scenery.
- **Interior (Grand Foyer):**
    - Unbelievably opulent: colored marble, paintings, mosaics, chandeliers.
    - Grand, sweeping staircase with curvilinear arabesques.
    - Engaged columns, pilasters, and a painted ceiling.
- **Layout:**
    - The **front half** of the building is dedicated to social spaces (foyers, staircases) where the bourgeoisie could see and be seen.
    - Private boxes are separated by walls and doorways, emphasizing **individual bourgeois experience**.
    - Balconies offer varied, radical viewpoints of the interior space from multiple angles.


### Context

- **Connection to Haussmannization:**
    - The Opéra was a crown jewel of **Napoleon III's reconstruction of Paris**, led by Baron Haussmann.
    - Haussmann's broad boulevards were designed to create a **city of spectacle**—and the Opéra stands as the ultimate monument to that vision.
    - From the Opéra's balconies, one sees the very boulevards that appear in Impressionist paintings, linking urban planning to modern art.
- **Function:**
    - Housed both opera and ballet (ballet continues there today; opera has since moved).
    - The building itself is as much a **stage for society** as it is a theater—a space where the newly modernized bourgeoisie could display their status.
- **Art Historical Significance:**
    - The Opéra represents the **public, formal face** of Haussmann's Paris.
    - Understanding its opulence helps us appreciate the **radicality of Degas**:
        - While Garnier celebrated the spectacle of the audience, Degas turned his gaze **backstage**—to rehearsal rooms, dancers waiting, and chaperones.
        - He rejected the polished public spaces in favor of unidealized, intimate moments.
- **Connection to Impressionism:**
    - Cassatt and other Impressionists also depicted the **audience as spectacle** (e.g., women in balconies), offering alternative viewpoints that reflect the modern experience of urban life.
- **Theme of Vision:**
    - The building's architecture—with its balconies, boxes, and varied sightlines—embodies a fascination with **seeing and being seen**, a central concern of modern life in the city Haussmann reshaped.

<!-- 

Claude Monet​ painted more than 30 canvases depicting Rouen cathedral between 1892 and 1894. 

**Over two late winters and early springs, 1892 and 1893, he rented a space across from the cathedral and painted the cathedral in different effects of light**. What he did was he had several canvases going at once, each for a different moment of the day and a different effect of light.

They’re meant to be seen together, and he exhibited them together. They’re very beautiful. One really does get the sense of optical effects of different times of day: the morning mist, the sun coming out, the heat of the afternoon sun.

Well, that makes sense. If Monet is trying to define this ephemeral quality of light, then as the sun moves, he would need to change canvases, he can’t paint that fast.

Then he would come back to it day after day, but in also different weather effects. Having this temporary studio across the street allowed him to paint in the rain, early in the morning, etc. There’s a lot of paint on these canvases, and so this is not something that was done quickly.

Monet was always interested in capturing **the fleeting effects of something** that he saw. Here, it’s become the exact subject of the painting. The irony is that as he’s capturing something that’s fleeting, he takes longer and longer to paint it and to finish it, not outside, but to finish it in the studio.

There’s another irony here, which is that if their subject is really about light and the way light constructs form, and I think that really is the subject, he’s picked a pretty potent thing to render that on. That is to say, a medieval cathedral, with all of its religious connotations, its historical connotations, and is solid in the extreme, and yet in the rendering by Monet, these are not such solid forms.


He’s choosing something very **identified with France, the Gothic style**. There feels to me like there’s something **nationalistic** here. There feels to me like there’s something poignant here. This is, in a sense, taking that grand history, taking all of the power that these function as symbolically, and in a sense understanding them through the lens of the late 19th century.

What happens to my eyes as I move across the canvases is different parts of the cathedral protrude and recede in different ways and different light, and in a sense the physical stone itself becomes this mutable experience in that the building is shaped and reshaped by the way that light hits it, and that the very architecture is transformed. In a sense, it is the triumph of the optical over the physical.

Which is something very different than the Gothic architects would have thought about the church, because what could be seen was really a symbol for what couldn’t be seen. In a way, what Monet seems to be telling us here in the end of the 19th century is what we see is what there is.

### Biography

- **Artist:** James McNeill Whistler (1834–1903).
- **Nationality:** American-born, but worked primarily in England and France.
- **Role:** A key figure in the Aesthetic Movement, which championed **"art for art's sake"** —the idea that art should be judged by its formal qualities, not its narrative or moral content.
- **Connection to Impressionism:** Whistler was associated with avant-garde circles in Paris and London. While not an Impressionist, he shared their interest in **modern subjects, urban life, and the effects of light**.



### Formal Description

- **Title:** *Nocturne in Black and Gold, the Falling Rocket* (1875).
- **Subject:** Fireworks display along the River Thames at night.
- **Composition:**
    - Form is largely obliterated by darkness; no definite shapes or clear narrative.
    - The viewer sees only the **sparkle of rocket remnants**—ephemeral bursts of gold against a dark sky.
- **Color Palette:** Dominated by black and gold, with subtle tonal variations.
- **Brushwork:** Loose and suggestive rather than descriptive. The painting prioritizes **atmosphere over illusion**.
- **Japanese Influence:** Whistler drew on Japanese printmaking, particularly Hiroshige's depictions of fireworks, for compositional ideas and the rejection of Western perspectival conventions.


### Context

- **Transition from Pissarro:**
    - Pissarro turned to the **rural outskirts** of Paris, finding meaning in modest landscapes and human encounter.
    - Whistler shifts to the **modern urban night**, but like Pissarro, he rejects traditional compositional clarity. Instead of an obstructed rural view, Whistler offers an **obliterated urban view**—where darkness dissolves form entirely.
- **The Nocturne Concept:**
    - "Nocturne" is a musical term (evoking nighttime compositions). Whistler used musical titles to emphasize that painting, like music, could be appreciated for its **rhythm, tone, and texture**—not for storytelling.
- **The Ruskin Trial (1878):**
    - Critic John Ruskin accused Whistler of "flinging a pot of paint in the public’s face"—a condemnation of the painting's apparent lack of craftsmanship.
    - Whistler sued for libel.
    - **Key exchange:**
        - Ruskin's lawyer: "Did it take you much time to paint this? The labor of two days is that for which you ask 200 guineas?"
        - Whistler: "No, I ask 200 guineas for the knowledge I have gained in the work of a lifetime."

    - The great art critic John Ruskin accused Whistler of “flinging a pot of paint in the public’s face.” Art was expected to show craftsmanship, to depict reality, to create illusions, to show the painter’s skill in creating those illusions.

    - Whistler aligned with the Aesthetic Movement, which rejected the expectation that art should tell a story, teach a moral, or depict recognizable reality.
    - Beauty could be found in **color, form, and tone alone**—a radical departure from academic tradition.


### Significance

- **Modernist Break:**
    - The painting abandons narrative, illusionism, and the expectation of recognizable forms.
    - It asks whether **color and tonal relationships alone** can create a profound emotional experience.
- **Urban Modernity:**
    - The painting engages with the **newly illuminated city**—a subject enabled by scientific advancement and urban development.
    - Night, once a time of darkness, was being "beaten back" by gaslight and electricity. Whistler captures this modern transformation.
- **Legacy:**
    - The Ruskin trial and Whistler's defense of his work marked a turning point in how art was understood and valued.
    - His emphasis on abstraction, musical analogy, and "art for art's sake" influenced later movements, including **Symbolism, Abstract Expressionism, and modernist formalism**.
- **Connection to Impressionism:**
    - Like the Impressionists, Whistler focused on **ephemeral effects** (fireworks, atmosphere, light) and **modern urban life**.
    - However, his move toward near-abstraction and his explicit rejection of narrative pushed avant-garde concerns further than many of his French contemporaries.
