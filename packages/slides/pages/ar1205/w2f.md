The art of High Renaissance is absolutely secular in its outlook; even in the representation of religious subjects, it attains its ideal style not by contrasting natural with supernatural reality, but by creating a distance between the objects of natural reality itself---a distance which in the world of visual experience creates differences of value similar to those that exist between the elite and the masses in human society. Its harmony is the utopian ideal of a world from which all conflict is excluded, and moreover, not as a result of the rule of a democratic but of an autocratic principle.

## Michelangelo's *David* (1501‚Äì1504)

### Historical Context

**Commission and Location**
- Commissioned for the **outside of Florence Cathedral** (Santa Maria del Fiore)
- Intended to be placed **40 feet above street level** ‚Äî explaining its monumental scale (17 feet tall)
- Michelangelo was only **26 years old** when he received this commission
- Commission followed his enormous success with the *Piet√†* in Rome
- Carved from an **abandoned block of marble** that had lain unused for decades

**Political Background**
- Florence had recently **expelled the Medici family** (1494)
- Republic was reestablished under the influence of **Savonarola**, a charismatic monk
- Savonarola created a **theocratic state**, famously burning "vanities" ‚Äî manuscripts, paintings, and secular art
- Savonarola was eventually **excommunicated, arrested, executed, and burned**
- Florence saw itself as having defeated **two tyrants**: the Medici and Savonarola
- The government looked back to the **golden age of the Florentine Republic** (100 years prior) for inspiration

### The David Symbol

**Biblical Story**
- David was a young shepherd when the Philistines attacked Israel
- No Israelite warrior would face the giant **Goliath**
- David volunteered, took off his armor, and used a sling with a single stone
- He struck Goliath between the eyes, felled him, and cut off his head with the giant's own sword
- Story of **good overcoming evil through God's favor**

**Florentine Identification**
- Florentines saw themselves as **underdogs like David**
- They believed they had defeated enemies through **God's favor**
- David became a **potent political symbol** of the Republic
- Earlier Davids by **Donatello and Verrocchio** also embodied these ideals
- Donatello's *David* had been commissioned by the Medici ‚Äî when they were expelled, the government **appropriated it** as a symbol of the Republic

### Artistic Innovation

**Classical Influence**
- Part of the **rediscovery of ancient Greek and Roman culture** under Medici patronage
- Artists sought to **compete with ancient models**
- The **contrapposto stance** (weight on right leg) comes directly from classical antiquity
- Florence saw itself as the **inheritor of Roman humanist traditions**

**A Revolutionary Choice of Moment**
- Earlier Davids show the **moment of victory** ‚Äî after the fight
- They appear **youthful, confident, contemplative, introspective**
- Michelangelo chooses the **moment before the battle**
- David looks across space, **readying himself for the fight ahead**
- This is a **"pregnant moment"** ‚Äî immediate anticipation of action

**Physical and Psychological Tension**
- Hips forward, shoulders forward, but head turned at **three-quarters**
- Eyes turned even further than the head ‚Äî just caught first glance of enemy
- The pose itself (contrapposto) is **relaxed**
- Body seems to have been at rest, now being **filled with tension** for battle
- Face conveys **uncertainty about the future**
- We sense him as a **human being facing his enemy**, not displaying confidence in God
- This makes the sculpture **reassuring** ‚Äî we know David will triumph, Florence will defeat its enemies

### Transformation of Meaning

**From Religious to Political**
- Originally commissioned for a **religious context** (cathedral exterior)
- Too fabulous to place high up on the cathedral
- Instead placed on a platform in front of the **seat of government** (Piazza della Signoria)
- Taken from religious context and placed in **political context**
- Became a **symbol of the newly reconstituted Republic**
- Medici supporters threw stones at it ‚Äî recognizing its **political potency**

**From Political to Artistic**
- Now in the **Accademia Gallery** ‚Äî a museum context
- Transformed again into a **work of art** in our secular museum culture
- Understood as expression of **history, aesthetic appreciation, and Michelangelo's mastery**
- Michelangelo earned the nickname **"Il Divino"** (the Divine One) for this almost superhuman achievement

### Key Takeaways

1. **Scale and location** were determined by original architectural context
2. **Political symbolism** was central to the sculpture's meaning for Florentines
3. **Choice of moment** (anticipation rather than victory) creates psychological tension
4. **Classical influence** appears in contrapposto stance and humanist ideals
5. **Meaning transformed** from religious ‚Üí political ‚Üí artistic over time
6. **Michelangelo's mastery** earned him divine status in the eyes of contemporaries
