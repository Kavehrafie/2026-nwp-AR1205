<!-- 

1848, the year of the French Revolution, was a watershed in the history of art. The barricades of the revolution were a popular subject for artists, and Horace Vernet's painting captures the chaos and violence of the moment. The painting depicts a group of revolutionaries defending a barricade on the Rue Soufflot in Paris, with smoke and debris filling the air. The figures are depicted in a realistic style, with attention to detail and a sense of immediacy that conveys the urgency of the situation. The painting is an example of how art can serve as a powerful tool for documenting historical events and conveying their emotional impact.
- after the 1848's fall of the monarchy (known as the "February Revolution"), the new government of the Second Republic was established, and artists were eager to capture the spirit of the times. The barricades of the revolution became a popular subject for artists.
- the republic however, was short-lived, and in 1852, Louis-Napoleon Bonaparte declared himself Emperor Napoleon III, ushering in the Second Empire. This period was marked by a return to conservative values and a focus on traditional subjects in art, which led to a decline in the popularity of Realism. However, the legacy of the 1848 revolution and its impact on art continued to be felt throughout the 19th century and beyond.

<!-- 

-  From thispoint of view it is simply Courbet’s insistence on contemporaneityas a necessary condition of the concrete that separates theacademic artist from the innovator.

Large paintings were supposed to be historical, heroic. Allegorical or religious, but this is a genre painting. This is a painting of everyday life, and according to the rules of the Academy, it had no business being on a canvas this large. (genre should be small, history should be large).

when Courbet submitted this painting to the jury, he submitted it as a history painting, and he must have considered it to be a history painting of our world now.

Courbet wanted to paint his own day, his own time. In fact, he said “An epoch can only be reproduced by its own artists. I hold the artists of one century basically incapable of reproducing the aspect of a past or future century.”

Many of them members of Courbet’s own family. This is the funeral of Courbet’s great uncle. To submit a painting to the jury of the salon, the jury that decided which paintings could be hung in the official exhibitions called salons, and not even say the name of the person who’s being buried, Courbet is definitely making a statement here.

For one thing, he’s bringing the experience of rural life into the capital of France and trying to bring it into the most elite environment for the arts, in effect heroicizing the ordinary, heroicizing common humanity.

Look what Courbet has put in the middle of this composition. The grave, but also the gravedigger. Courbet gives this simple laborer a kind of dignity that is completely unexpected in mid-19th-century painting.

Absurption:  That’s probably most clearly represented by the child at the extreme right edge of the canvas who seems completely involved in her own thoughts, suggesting a kind of interior experience that is central to Courbet’s belief.

<!-- 

Courbet has created a horizontal frieze in contrast to a vertically oriented canvas that we might expect in a Renaissance painting, where a figure would be assumed into heaven, where would see angels, where we would see the clouds parting, where we would see the possibility for redemption.

The divine is represented by a crucifix, which is carried by one of the clergymen, so the only sense of the divine is actually physical.

The painting is divided roughly into three groups. At the left is the clergy. In fact, we can see the pallbearers carrying the coffin in from the left. In front of the coffin, we can see the priest. Towards the center, town officials, and to their right, women mourning.

Each of these different types of figures were treated equally. One art historian has called this “democracy in painting.”

The faces of the figures and their poses have not been idealized. One of my favorite aspects of this painting is the hunting dog. In a traditionally composed painting, perhaps one for the salon, one would not allow a dog to be represented unless it functioned symbolically.

I think it’s a lack of a focal point, there’s no one place where our eye is drawn, that does give us that sense of the normal distractions of life, even at a funeral.

<!-- 

If we look closely at Courbet’s painting The Stonebreakers of 1849 (painted only one year after Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels wrote their influential pamphlet, The Communist Manifesto) the artist’s concern for the plight of the poor is evident. Here, two figures labor to break and remove stone from a road that is being built.  

Courbet wants to show what is “real,” and so he has depicted a man that seems too old and a boy that seems still too young for such back-breaking labor. This is not meant to be heroic: it is meant to be an accurate account of the abuse and deprivation that was a common feature of mid-century French rural life.
