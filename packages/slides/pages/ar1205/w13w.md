## Post-War Art Movements
#### WEEK 13


:: note ::

April 1, 2026

<!--
Today we cross the second watershed: World War II. The atomic bomb and the Holocaust shattered the already fragile faith in progress that World War I had begun to undo. Every movement we cover today is a response to the question: what can art possibly do after Auschwitz, after Hiroshima?

**Road map:** We start with Ben Shahn's Social Realism — art that confronts inequality head-on. Then Hopper's urban alienation, where the modern city becomes a stage for loneliness. Next comes Abstract Expressionism — Pollock and Frankenthaler — where artists abandon representation entirely, turning inward to gesture, the unconscious, and the raw truth of materials. We end with Mies van der Rohe's Seagram Building, where modernist architecture reaches its purified extreme.

- Shahn (Social Realism) → Hopper (alienation) → Pollock (Abstract Expressionism / action painting) → Frankenthaler (stain painting / Color Field) → Mies van der Rohe (International Style architecture)
- Keep asking students: what problem is each artist or movement trying to solve? Is the answer to look harder at reality, or to abandon reality altogether?
-->

<!--
Ben Shahn painted *Contemporary American Sculpture* in 1940, inspired by the Whitney Annual Exhibition — a real gallery showing eight sculptures in the show. But look at the walls: through doorways and windows, the gallery opens onto scenes of rural poverty — a West Virginia mining town, an Arkansas farmer, two African American women outside a New York welfare hospital — all based on photographs Shahn took while working for the WPA. The tiled gallery floor turns into dirt and grass through the doorway on the left. Shahn is asking: what does high modernist sculpture have to say about real Americans’ lives? The X-form of the crutches echoes the X-form of an abstracted bronze body on a pedestal — a visual argument that pristine, idealized bodies in galleries have nothing to say about the suffering he witnessed. This is a hinge painting: Shahn transitioning from Social Realism toward what he called a “personal realism.”

**Transition to Hopper:** Shahn confronts social inequality directly — the real world bursting into the art gallery. Hopper takes a different path: instead of demanding that art engage with society, he paints the loneliness that modern society produces.

- **WPA context:** the Works Progress Administration employed artists during the New Deal to document American life — Shahn traveled the country as a photographer
- **The collision:** a pristine gallery of modernist sculpture invaded by the poverty outside — the art world vs. the real world
- **Photographic sources:** Shahn’s own WPA photographs — West Virginia mining town (left), Ozarks farmer (center), welfare hospital in NYC (right)
- **The doorway trick:** gallery tile becomes dirt and grass — the boundary between art and life dissolves
- **Visual rhyme:** the crutches’ X-form mirrors the abstract bronze body on the pedestal — what does high art say about real suffering?
- **Hinge moment:** moving from Social Realism (art for social good) to “personal realism” — the artist reckoning with his own role
-->

![Edward Hopper, Nighthawks, 1942, oil on canvas, 84.1 x 152.4 cm (33-1/8 x 60 inches)](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Nighthawks_by_Edward_Hopper_1942.jpg/3840px-Nighthawks_by_Edward_Hopper_1942.jpg)


<!--
Painted in 1942 at the height of World War II, Edward Hopper's *Nighthawks* is the definitive American image of urban alienation. Through the glass window of a corner diner, we observe four figures bathed in warm, artificial light — yet we have no access to them. There is no visible door. The street is eerily silent, the buildings above abandoned. Hopper strips away all nonessential detail, leaving a scene that is both specific (cherry-topped stools, coffee urns with visible liquid levels, a five-cent cigar sign) and generalized (the street corner is nowhere in particular — it could be any American city). The painting invites us to invent narratives while offering only one certainty: we are outside looking in, alone with the sound of our own footsteps.

**Transition to Pollock:** Hopper paints alienation with realist precision — you can identify every object. Pollock will take the opposite path: abandoning representation entirely, turning away from the visible world toward the inner life of gesture and the unconscious.

- **No visible door:** the viewer is physically locked out. The painting is a glass cage — we can look but cannot enter or hear
- **Composition:** strict horizontal and vertical lines (counter, window frames, buildings), one sharp diagonal leading the eye to the abandoned cash register across the street
- **Light:** warm fluorescent interior spills outward onto the sidewalk in complex, layered rays. Hopper meticulously renders how light hits the curved glass — the contrast between glowing interior and dark empty street heightens the separation
- **Detail vs. abstraction:** specific objects (napkin holders, salt shakers, glass straws in coffee urns) but an unidentifiable street — we know we've been there, yet it exists nowhere
- **Wartime context:** painted in 1942 when the war's outcome was uncertain, American cities felt emptied out, and the national mood was fear and waiting
- **The four figures:** proximity without connection — the couple and the lone man share space but not conversation. Together, alone
- **The pulled blinds (upper windows):** someone was here, but now they're gone — asleep, moved away, or lost to war
- **Ask students:** Who are these people? Why are they here so late? The painting refuses to answer — and that refusal is the point
-->

### Abstract Expressionism ✏️
</Box>

<!--
Painted in 1950, Jackson Pollock's *Autumn Rhythm (Number 30)* is a monumental 17-foot-wide canvas that abandons all tradition of naturalism. Made with house paint and enamel dripped, flung, and poured onto raw, unprimed canvas laid flat on his studio floor in East Hampton, the work registers the artist's physical movement through time and space — a record of gesture, rhythm, and spontaneous invention. Pollock sought to bypass the conscious, rational mind, which the horrors of WWII and the atomic bomb had rendered suspect, and access something more elemental. Like a jazz musician improvising on mastered skill, he let the unconscious guide his hand. The surface becomes what he called an "arena to act in" — paint seeps into the weave, cracks, pools, and arcs, offering infinite variety of texture and a profound break with centuries of refinement.

**Transition to Pollock photo:** The next slide shows a famous photograph of Pollock in action, alongside a quote from critic Harold Rosenberg that defines what "Action Painting" really means.

- **Materials:** house paint and black enamel from the hardware store, not the academy — a deliberate rejection of fine-art refinement
- **Raw canvas:** no gesso, no sealant — paint seeps directly into the weave, staining rather than sitting on top
- **Technique (Action Painting):** no brush touched the canvas. Pollock moved *over* it, letting paint fall, drip, splash, and arc. Long trails record large rhythmic arm motions — gravity as co-author
- **Up close:** infinite variety — soft stains, sharp dots, cracked paint, matte vs. shiny passages. Pull back and it becomes unified rhythm
- **All-over composition:** no focal point, no hierarchy, no figure vs. ground — a democratic visual field where every inch is equally important
- **Post-war context:** painted five years after WWII, during the Cold War. The Holocaust revealed, the atomic bomb looming. Artists felt naturalism was no longer viable — how do you paint a body after the camps?
- **Influences:** Surrealism (the unconscious), Dada (rejection of rationality), Mexican muralists (large scale), jazz (improvisation on mastered skill)
- **The title:** originally just "Number 30" — *Autumn Rhythm* was added later, suggesting seasonal change and musical tempo
- **Cold War weaponization (Doss):** the US government covertly promoted Abstract Expressionism as proof of American creative freedom vs. Soviet repression — Pollock's liberation became a propaganda tool
- **Art market (Doss):** the Met purchased *Autumn Rhythm* for $30,000 in 1957; New York replaced Paris as capital of modern art; 30 galleries in 1950 became 300 by 1960
- **Who got left out (Doss):** the AbEx canon was constructed as a "virile, all-male club" — Norman Lewis (African American AbEx painter, showing at premier venues), Lee Krasner, Louise Bourgeois, and Helen Frankenthaler were systematically marginalized
-->

Hans Namuth's famous photograph captures Pollock in the act — pouring paint over a canvas laid on the floor of his East Hampton studio. **Transition to Frankenthaler:** Pollock's gesture is aggressive, performative, physical. Next we'll see Frankenthaler take the same raw-canvas innovation in a quieter direction — stain painting, where the artist's hand nearly disappears.

- **The photograph:** Namuth shot Pollock from below the canvas, making the artist loom over his work — the image that launched a thousand myths about tortured genius
- **Rosenberg's argument:** the canvas is no longer a picture to complete but an "arena in which to act" — the painting is the event, not the object
- **"Just TO PAINT":** liberation from political, aesthetic, and moral value — the gesture itself is the meaning
- **This defined "Action Painting":** the term that separated Pollock and de Kooning from the quieter color-field painters like Rothko
- **Mythologizing the artist (Doss):** Namuth's photograph, published in *Artnews* in 1951 ("Pollock Paints a Picture"), cast AbEx as a "virile, violent, all-male club" — equivalent to brooding 1950s movie stars like Marlon Brando and James Dean. The myth of the lone male genius became the movement's public face

#### Stain Painting ✏️
</div>

<!--
Painted in 1952, Helen Frankenthaler's *Mountains and Sea* is the first major stain painting — a technique she pioneered by pouring thinned paint directly onto raw, unprimed canvas, allowing color to seep into the weave like fresco. Inspired by a trip to Cape Breton, Nova Scotia, the canvas hovers between landscape and pure abstraction: a blue horizon line on the right suggests sea and sky, while the center and left dissolve into amorphous pools, biomorphic charcoal lines, and splashes that resist literal reading. Unlike the gestural drama of Pollock or de Kooning, Frankenthaler's stain technique subdues the artist's hand, embedding color within the fabric itself and asserting the flat, two-dimensional truth of the painted surface. The work is both an intuitive response to nature and a brave intersection of conflicting ideas — abstraction versus reference, spontaneity versus material control.

**Transition to studio photo:** This next slide shows the painting alongside Frankenthaler in her studio — notice the scale of the canvas and how she worked on the floor, like Pollock, but with a very different relationship to gesture.

- **Stain painting innovation:** thinned paint poured onto raw canvas, color seeps into the weave — embedded, not sitting on top. Similar to fresco: the color becomes one with the surface
- **Between landscape and abstraction:** the right side has a recognizable blue horizon (sea meeting sky), but the center and left fall apart into pure color and line — the landscape is a trigger, not a subject
- **Charcoal lines:** biomorphic, organic, body-like shapes drawn on top of the stained fields — they don't align with the color, creating independent systems that coexist
- **Subduing the hand:** unlike Pollock's drips (which declare "I was here!"), the stain is impersonal — a meditative, optical experience where the viewer sinks into color rather than reading gesture
- **Greenberg context:** critic Clement Greenberg championed Pollock and theorized that modern art progresses toward flatness. But Frankenthaler was not illustrating his theories — she was responding directly to canvas and landscape
- **Legacy:** *Mountains and Sea* directly influenced Morris Louis and Kenneth Noland, who visited her studio and launched the Washington Color School (Color Field painting)
- **Gender:** Frankenthaler was 23 when she made this — a woman in a male-dominated movement whose innovation was often overshadowed by the myth of Pollock's drip
- **Gender exclusions (Doss):** the AbEx canon systematically excluded women — Lee Krasner changed her name from "Lenore" to "Lee" as "a blind to her gender" and signed with initials "L.K."; Louise Bourgeois was known primarily as "the wife of art historian Robert Goldwater." Krasner recalled: "I didn't fit in" — excluded despite equal achievement
-->

Now we shift to architecture, where Mies van der Rohe pursues the same purification at the scale of a building: stripping structure to its essentials, making glass and steel into an ethical statement about honesty and order.

- **Scale:** the canvases dominate the studio — she needed room to move around and pour from multiple angles

- **Contrast with Pollock's studio:** same horizontal orientation, but Frankenthaler's process is meditative where Pollock's was theatrical
- **Gender and the canon (Doss):** Namuth's photographs mythologized Pollock as the lone hero; no such mythology was built for Frankenthaler, whose innovation was often attributed to Greenberg's influence rather than her own vision

### International Style Architecture ✏️
</Box>

<!--
The Seagram Building (1956–58) is Ludwig Mies van der Rohe's masterpiece of corporate modernism and the purest expression of the International Style in America. The building doesn't hug the sidewalk like its neighbors — Mies set it back 100 feet from Park Avenue, creating a open granite plaza that became a new kind of public space in midtown Manhattan. The bronze-and-glass tower rises with absolute regularity: identical bays of amber-tinted glass and dark bronze mullions from bottom to top. Crucially, the visible bronze I-beams on the exterior are not structural — they're applied to the surface to express the idea of structure. This is Mies's paradox: the building is honest about its materials (you can see what it's made of) but theatrical about its structure (the "bones" on display are decorative). Together with Philip Johnson, who designed the interiors and the Four Seasons restaurant, Mies created a building that made modernist architecture synonymous with corporate power and cultural sophistication.

**Transition to Oldenburg / Neo-Dada:** Mies pushed modernism to its purified extreme — and that extreme provoked a backlash. The Neo-Dada and Pop artists we'll see next rejected the sincerity and austerity of both Abstract Expressionism and the International Style, turning instead toward irony, everyday objects, and mass culture.

- **Setback from the street:** Mies refused to build to the lot line, instead creating a public plaza — unprecedented for a corporate tower, now copied everywhere
- **Materials:** bronze, amber-tinted glass, travertine and granite plaza — expensive materials used with restraint
- **The structural paradox:** the visible bronze I-beams on the facade are non-structural — applied to express the idea of structure. Honest materials, theatrical structure
- **Philip Johnson:** collaborated on the project, designing the interior spaces including the landmark Four Seasons restaurant
- **Corporate modernism:** the Seagram Building made the International Style the visual language of corporate America — modernist austerity as a symbol of power and sophistication
- **Legacy:** this building changed how skyscrapers look — every glass tower with visible mullions owes something to Mies
- **Connection to Le Corbusier:** both sought a universal architectural language, but where Le Corbusier's Villa Savoye was domestic and utopian, the Seagram Building is corporate and monumental
-->

### Neo-Dada and Pop Art ✏️
</Box>


<!--
Put these two side by side and the confrontation becomes unmistakable. Pollock's drip — the emblem of authentic, inward gesture — is replicated by Rauschenberg over his actual bed. - **Side-by-side comparison:** the drips in Rauschenberg's Bed are a deliberate quotation of Pollock's technique — but applied to a real quilt instead of raw canvas
- **The inversion:** Pollock's gesture = the inner life of the artist made visible. Rauschenberg's gesture = "You really believe that? Here's my actual bed"
- **Modernism vs. postmodernism:** one is earnest, the other is self-aware. The shift happens in the five years between these two works
- **Declaration of independence (Doss):** two years before *Bed*, Rauschenberg erased a drawing by Willem de Kooning — the *Erased de Kooning Drawing* (1953) was his literal declaration of independence from Abstract Expressionism

<!--
Robert Rauschenberg’s *Bed* (1955) is a “combine” — his term for works that are neither painting nor sculpture but somewhere in between, made from real bedding: his own quilt, pillow, and sheets, pulled from the horizontal and mounted vertically on the wall, then attacked with pencil, paint, and drips. It’s a direct confrontation with Pollock, who was still the dominant figure when this was made. Pollock’s drip had become an emblem of authentic, inward experience; Rauschenberg replicates that drip over his actual bed — the real arena of dreams — and in doing so, turns sincerity into irony. This is the hinge moment where American art shifts from modernist earnestness to postmodern self-awareness: art that knows it’s art, and wants you in on the joke.

**Transition to Johns:** Rauschenberg put his private life on the wall. Johns takes a different approach — he takes a public symbol, the American flag, and asks whether it’s a painting or an object. Both artists are working the same territory: the border between art and life.

- **The combine:** Rauschenberg’s term for works between painting and sculpture — neither one, not quite the other. His motto: work “in the gap between art and life”
- **Real bedding:** his own quilt, pillowcase, sheets — taken from horizontal (where you sleep) and mounted vertical (where you look at art). The everyday becomes the aesthetic
- **Confronting Pollock:** the drips are a deliberate quotation. Pollock’s drip = authentic inner experience. Rauschenberg’s drip = ironic commentary on that claim. “You want the arena of dreams? Here’s my actual bed.”
- **Tongue-in-cheek:** this is not sincere Expressionism — it’s self-aware, playful, absurdist. The Oedipal relationship between a younger artist and his Abstract Expressionist elders
- **1955 as hinge:** the moment when American art moves from modernist sincerity to postmodern irony — from “I mean this” to “I’m quoting this”
- **Neo-Dada connection:** picks up Duchamp’s readymade logic — the bed is a found object, the drips are a found style — both repurposed with wit rather than reverence
- **Personal coding (Doss):** Rauschenberg’s combines also carried private meaning — his relationship with Jasper Johns and his experience as a gay man in repressive postwar America were encoded in works like *Odalisk* (1955–58), which parodied the exotic courtesan tradition of Ingres and Matisse
-->

<!--
Jasper Johns painted *Flag* in 1954–55 — before Pop Art existed — using encaustic (hot wax mixed with pigment), an ancient Egyptian technique, over a collage of torn newspaper strips mounted on plywood. The result walks a razor’s edge: is this a painting of a flag, or is it a flag? Johns deliberately chose a subject where composition and color are already fixed — the design is given, not invented. Yet it’s handmade, layered, textured — the lumpy translucent wax reveals fragments of newsprint underneath, the debris of political life embedded in the symbol itself. Johns called it a “thing the mind already knows” — an image so familiar we stop seeing it, until his surface forces us to look again. It functions as a mirror: every viewer brings their own relationship to the flag, and the painting holds all those meanings without endorsing any of them.

**Transition to detail slide:** The next slide shows a close-up of the surface so you can see the encaustic technique and the newspaper fragments embedded beneath the wax.

- **Encaustic:** ancient Egyptian technique — hot beeswax mixed with pigment. Johns revived it because it dries instantly, preserving every gesture, and it’s translucent — you can see through it to the layers below
- **Newspaper collage:** torn strips of newsprint are embedded under the wax — you can’t read a continuous story, but you can see the texture of political language buried inside the national symbol
- **”Is it a painting or is it a flag?”:** the question critics asked. It’s both — a representation and an object, walking the edge between Duchamp’s readymade and traditional painting
- **”Thing the mind already knows”:** Johns’s own phrase — he chose subjects so familiar they’re invisible, then made them visible again through surface and material
- **What he gives up:** color and composition are predetermined by the flag’s design. The creativity is in the surface, the material, the act of making — not in the invention of form
- **Before Pop Art:** this predates Warhol and Lichtenstein. Johns is the bridge between Abstract Expressionism and Pop — taking the handmade seriousness of the former and pointing it at the everyday imagery of the latter
- **McCarthyism (Doss):** made at the height of anti-communist hysteria in 1954–55, *Flag* questions the sanctity of American symbols in an era when patriotism was policed and conformity was enforced — the buried newsprint is the literal debris of political life fossilized inside the national emblem
-->
