## Constructivism

<!--
Constructivism emerged in Russia around 1915, founded by Vladimir Tatlin and Alexander Rodchenko. Unlike the introspective abstraction of Kandinsky or Malevich, Constructivists wanted art to serve the revolution — to build, not just to express. They worked with industrial materials like steel, glass, and iron, treating the artwork less as a contemplative object and more as a functional prototype for a new society.

**Transition from Malevich's Suprematism (previous session):** Malevich sought pure spiritual feeling through abstraction. Tatlin and Rodchenko asked: what good is pure feeling when people need housing, tools, and a rebuilt world?

- Key artists: Tatlin, Rodchenko, Naum Gabo, Antoine Pevsner, El Lissitzky
- Industrial materials, geometry, engineering logic — art as construction, not representation
- Born from the same revolutionary energy as Suprematism, but with a utilitarian turn
- Art should serve the proletariat, not sit in a gallery
-->

## Neo-Plasticism & De Stijl

<!--
De Stijl ("The Style") began in the Netherlands in 1917 as a circle of abstract artists, architects, and designers who believed that visual harmony could help rebuild a shattered world. Piet Mondrian's Neoplasticism — the movement's painting philosophy — reduced art to vertical and horizontal lines, primary colors, and non-representational arrangements of form, seeking a universal visual language that transcended individual subject matter. The group included Mondrian, Theo van Doesburg, Bart van der Leck, and Gerrit Rietveld, and they sought laws of equilibrium applicable to both art and life.

**Transition from WWI:** The war destroyed European civilization. De Stijl's response: strip everything down to elemental forms and rebuild from scratch. Pure order as an antidote to pure chaos.

- Founded 1917 in the Netherlands — neutral during WWI, but deeply affected by it
- Mondrian's Neoplasticism: only vertical/horizontal lines, primary colors, no naturalistic reference
- The goal was not aesthetic minimalism but utopian — visual balance as a model for social harmony
- Key names: Mondrian, van Doesburg, van der Leck, Rietveld
- Applied across painting, architecture, furniture (Rietveld's Red Blue Chair)
- Art history lineage: grew out of Cubist fragmentation, pushed toward complete abstraction
-->

---

![Piet Mondrian, Composition No. II, with Red and Blue, oil on canvas, 1929 (original date partly obliterated; mistakenly repainted 1925 by Mondrian), oil on canvas, 40.3 x 32.1 cm](https://www.moma.org/media/W1siZiIsIjI5MjUwNiJdLFsicCIsImNvbnZlcnQiLCItcXVhbGl0eSA5MCAtcmVzaXplIDIwMDB4MjAwMFx1MDAzZSJdXQ.jpg?sha=202393cbe411d77b)

<!--
Painted in 1929, Mondrian's *Composition No. II, with Red and Blue* reduces painting to its absolute essentials: black horizontal and vertical lines, white planes, and two primary colors. This is not just aesthetic minimalism — it is a utopian proposition. Mondrian believed that if art could model visual balance and harmony, perhaps that equilibrium could translate into politics and daily life. The surface is not mechanical — look closely and you'll see visible brushstrokes and facture, a human touch that softens the geometric severity.

**Transition to Purism:** Mondrian sought universal harmony through pure abstraction. Next we'll see Le Corbusier and Ozenfant pursue a similar universal order — but through the vocabulary of the machine age rather than the grid.

- **Post-WWI utopianism:** Mondrian believed art had agency — the power to help rebuild society through visual harmony
- **The palette is deliberate:** only primary colors (red, blue) plus black, white, gray — no mixed hues, nothing derived from nature
- **The grid acts like stained glass armature** — holding color in place, simultaneously creating and denying spatial depth
- **Surface matters:** visible brushstrokes remind us this is handmade, not a mechanical print — "this is a made thing"
- **Push-pull dynamics:** blue tends to push forward, red can recede — the painting breathes despite its austerity
- **Art historical path here:** Mondrian started with naturalistic landscapes, moved through Cubist fragmentation (his apple tree series), and arrived at pure abstraction — the space between branches became as important as the branches themselves
- **At MoMA:** you walk from Monet's immersive Water Lilies into this room of spare geometry — the contrast is deliberate and instructive
- **Ask students:** Can you just feel it, or do you need to analyze it? Answer: both work
-->

### Purism

:: right ::

![ Amédée Ozenfant, Guitar and Bottles, 1920. Oil on canvas, 81 x 99.7 cm.](https://www.guggenheim.org/wp-content/uploads/1920/01/76.2553.24_ph_web-1.jpg)

:: left ::

![ Le Corbusier (Charles-Édouard Jeanneret), Still Life, 1920. Oil on canvas, 81 x 99.7 cm.](https://www.moma.org/media/W1siZiIsIjE2MTc2NSJdLFsicCIsImNvbnZlcnQiLCItcXVhbGl0eSA5MCAtcmVzaXplIDIwMDB4MjAwMFx1MDAzZSJdXQ.jpg?sha=58e078897fd53311)


<!--
Purism was developed around 1918 by the architect-painter Charles-Édouard Jeanneret — better known by his pseudonym Le Corbusier — and the painter Amédée Ozenfant. In their 1918 manifesto *After Cubism*, they argued that Cubism had degenerated into mere decorative elaboration. Their alternative: a painting of clean, architectural structure — simple forms inspired by mass-produced industrial objects like guitars, bottles, and mechanical parts. Purism shared De Stijl's faith in universal order, but found its vocabulary not in the grid and primary colors, but in the geometry of the machine.

**Transition from Mondrian:** Mondrian stripped painting down to elemental abstraction. Le Corbusier and Ozenfant took a different path to the same goal: instead of eliminating the object, they purified it — making the everyday industrial world the subject of a new classical clarity.

- **After Cubism (1918):** their manifesto attacked late Cubism as decorative; sought a new classical order based on industrial forms
- **Subject matter:** guitars, bottles, pipes, mechanical elements — but rendered with geometric clarity, not Cubist fragmentation
- **Le Corbusier** (1887–1965): Swiss-born, worked in Paris — architect, painter, urban planner, and theorist all at once
- **Ozenfant** (1886–1966): painter who later settled in the US, turned to teaching and mural painting
- **Painting as manifesto:** both artists used painting to communicate their ideals to a sophisticated audience, but their deepest impact was in architecture and design
- **The irony:** their utopian vision appealed mainly to wealthy clients, not the workers and social organizers for whom it was intended
- **Compare to De Stijl:** same postwar impulse toward universal order, but Purism finds beauty in the machine rather than in pure abstraction
-->

<!--
The Villa Savoye (1929) is the fullest realization of Le Corbusier’s famous dictum: “The house is a machine for living in.” Located just outside Paris, it embodies his Five Points of Architecture — a universal system he devised by 1926 that could be applied to any site. **Transition from Purism paintings:** The still lifes on the previous slide purified everyday objects into geometric clarity. The Villa Savoye does the same thing at the scale of a building — stripping architecture down to its functional essence and finding beauty in that discipline.

- **Five Points of Architecture (1926):** pilotis (columns raising the building), roof terrace (garden in the sky), free plan (no load-bearing walls inside), free façade (smooth wall for formal experimentation), ribbon windows (horizontal light)

- **Wealthy clients, utopian ideals:** the Savoye family commissioned it as a weekend retreat — the irony of a machine-age aesthetic serving bourgeois leisure
- **Legacy:** Villa Savoye became one of the most influential buildings of the 20th century, shaping modernist architecture worldwide

## Dadaism

<!--
Dada was an anti-art movement born in Zurich in 1916, in the middle of World War I, as a deliberate rejection of the rationality and bourgeois values that had led Europe into catastrophic violence. Dadaists embraced nonsense, absurdity, and provocation — collage, performance, readymades, and satire became their weapons. The movement spread to Berlin, Cologne, Paris, and New York, with key figures including Hugo Ball, Tristan Tzara, Marcel Duchamp, Francis Picabia, Hans Arp, and Kurt Schwitters.

**Transition from Purism/Le Corbusier:** Where Mondrian and Le Corbusier responded to the war by building visual and architectural order, Dada responded by tearing meaning apart. If rational civilization produced the trenches, then rational art was complicit. Dada's answer: make nonsense. Even the name "Dada" is baby talk — a deliberate rejection of meaning.

- Founded in Zurich 1916 at Cabaret Voltaire — a haven for wartime exiles
- Anti-art: rejected logic, beauty, and tradition because they had produced catastrophe
- Strategies: collage, photomontage, readymades, performance, deliberate provocation
- Key figures: Ball, Tzara, Duchamp, Picabia, Arp, Schwitters, Hausmann, Höch
- Berlin Dada was the most politically aggressive variant — directly targeting Weimar complacency
- Dada cleared the ground for Surrealism: once you've destroyed old rules, you can discover new ones
-->

---

![Raoul Hausmann, Spirit of the Age: Mechanical Head, 1919, wooden mannequin head with attached objects, 32.5 x 21 x 20 cm](https://res.cloudinary.com/image-solar/image/upload/v1775635565/2026nwp-ar1205/40764094980_44a60c4af9_k_bbdcyj.jpg)

<!--
Created in 1919, one year after World War I ended, Raoul Hausmann's *Mechanical Head* is a defining work of Berlin Dada. Attached to a blank, soulless mannequin head are found objects that replace human senses and thought: a typewriter cylinder and camera dials for ears, a ruler and tape measure across the forehead, a collapsible tin cup engraved with a tiny heart on top, and a crocodile wallet at the back of the skull. The number 22 replaces identity — evoking a soldier's dog tag or a bureaucratic file. Hausmann is saying: this is the modern human. Media plugged directly into the skull, science measuring everything but understanding nothing, and money quietly driving all thought from behind.

**Transition from Dada introduction:** This is what Berlin Dada looks like in practice — not abstract nonsense, but a precise, angry portrait of dehumanization. The title *Spirit of the Age* is deeply ironic.

- **Blank mannequin head:** no identity, no soul — one of millions of interchangeable soldiers
- **Type cylinder + camera dials (ears):** information fed directly into the mind, no critical filtering — media bypasses consciousness
- **Ruler + tape measure (forehead):** science's failed promise to control nature — in 1919, this hope lay in ruins
- **Number "22":** you are not a person, you are a number — a bureaucratic file, a soldier's tag
- **Tin cup with tiny heart (top):** the only trace of humanity, and it's collapsible, cheap, replaceable
- **Crocodile wallet (back of skull):** capitalism as the hidden driver of thought and war
- **Today's parallel:** ask students how this relates to social media algorithms feeding information without conscious thought
- **Key question for discussion:** Has technology made us more free, or more like Hausmann's mechanical head?
-->

<!--
Paul Klee's *Twittering Machine* (1922) is a small, delicate watercolor and ink drawing that manages to be simultaneously whimsical and unsettling. Four bird-like creatures perch on a hand-cranked mechanical apparatus, their wire legs attached to a central pivot. When you turn the crank, the whole assembly would spin — but what comes out isn't music, it's cacophony. Each bird produces a different visual "sound": spirals, jagged spikes, warbling curves — a synesthetic notation of noise, not song. Klee inverts the Swiss cuckoo clock tradition: instead of a single bird emerging on schedule from a neat wooden box, these creatures are freed from their housing, producing chaotic, ungovernable racket. Made at the Bauhaus in 1922, the drawing is both playful counterpoint to Bauhaus rationalism and a darker metaphor for a fragile society running on broken mechanics.

**Transition from Hausmann:** Where Hausmann rages against dehumanization with aggression, Klee responds with mischief and ambiguity. Both are Dada-adjacent, but Klee's weapon is humor — the birds seem to be having fun even as the machine threatens to fall apart.

- **Medium:** Pen and ink over watercolor wash — delicate, calligraphic line with atmospheric background
- **Synesthesia:** Klee translates sound into visual marks — spirals (boing), jagged spikes (squawks), curves (warbles) — the title "Twittering" is ironic; this is noise, not song
- **The cuckoo clock inverted:** Swiss tradition = one bird, one sound, on schedule. Klee = multiple birds, chaos, no timekeeping. A subversion of Swiss precision and Bauhaus order
- **"Taking a line for a walk":** Klee's famous pedagogical principle — the wires walk up the page, and the viewer's eye follows the implied motion of the crank
- **Bauhaus context:** Made while Klee was teaching at the Bauhaus, but this is the other side of the school — not Constructivist geometry but Expressionist play and Dada absurdity
- **Shadow beneath the play:** 1922 Germany = hyperinflation, political instability. The rickety machine producing only noise reads as a metaphor for a fragile society
- **Scale:** Only 25 x 19 inches — intimate, not monumental. You have to lean in close
-->

## Surrealism

<!--
Surrealism was a literary, philosophical, and artistic movement that explored dreams, the unconscious, and irrational juxtapositions. It began in the 1920s, anchored by André Breton's 1924 manifesto in Paris, which drew heavily on Freudian psychoanalysis. Where Dada destroyed meaning, Surrealism sought to discover new meaning by bypassing the rational mind entirely — through automatism (letting the hand move without conscious control), dream imagery, and uncanny combinations of unrelated objects.

**Transition from Dada/Klee:** Dada tore down the old rules. Surrealism asked: what do we find when we stop filtering? The unconscious becomes the new frontier — the last untouched territory of authentic human experience.

- Founded formally with Breton's 1924 Surrealist Manifesto in Paris
- Rooted in Freud: the unconscious mind as the true engine of creativity
- Two key methods: automatism (spontaneous, unfiltered creation) and dream imagery (irrational juxtapositions rendered with precision)
- Key artists: Dalí, Magritte, Miró, Max Ernst, Meret Oppenheim, Frida Kahlo
- Dada cleared the ground; Surrealism planted new seeds
- The movement spans painting, sculpture, film, literature, and photography
-->

---

![Salvador Dalí, Metamorphosis of Narcissus, 1937, oil on canvas, 51.1 x 78.1 cm ](https://res.cloudinary.com/image-solar/image/upload/v1775636124/2026nwp-ar1205/salvador_dali-metamorphosis_of_narcissus-1937-trivium-art-history_hkbc6m.jpg)

<!--
Painted in 1937, Salvador Dalí's *Metamorphosis of Narcissus* is a masterwork of Surrealist double-imagery. The painting depicts the Greek myth through a visual doubling: on the left, the kneeling figure of Narcissus gazes into a pool, his body stone-like; on the right, that identical form transforms into a hand emerging from the earth, holding an egg from which a narcissus flower hatches. One outline is two things at once — the knee becomes the thumb, the head becomes the egg, and the crack in the egg is simultaneously the shadow of the flower. Dalí renders all of this with meticulous academic precision, embodying his "paranoiac-critical method": using the technique of the rational mind to record the irrational visions of the unconscious.

**Transition from Surrealism intro:** This is the Dalí approach — unlike Miró's loose, automatic abstraction, Dalí wants both: the inspiration of the irrational delivered through the technique of the rational.

- **The doubling:** Narcissus kneeling (left) and a hand holding an egg (right) share the exact same outline — look back and forth and the shape flips
- **The myth:** Narcissus fell in love with his own reflection, wasted away, and a flower grew where he died — Dalí captures death transforming into rebirth
- **Paranoiac-critical method:** Dalí's term for deliberately seeing one thing as another and painting it so we see it too — a "willful misreading of Freud"
- **Academic precision:** unlike Miró's automatism (loose, abstract), Dalí paints impossible dream scenes with the technique of a Renaissance master
- **Freudian framework:** where Freud said the conscious mind filters the unconscious, Dalí claimed he could operate both simultaneously
- **Ask students:** can you see the doubling? How long does it take for the two shapes to merge into one?
- **Key quote:** "One form being one thing and at the same moment another"
-->

### readymade ✏️
</div>

<!--
Created in 1936, Meret Oppenheim's *Object* — a teacup, saucer, and spoon wrapped in Chinese gazelle fur — became an instant icon of Surrealism by colliding two things that don't belong together. The cool, hard porcelain of polite society meets warm, soft fur of the animal body, creating immediate visceral repulsion: imagine wet fur on your tongue, liquid seeping through. But the Surrealists prized exactly this Freudian tension — the conscious mind recoils while the unconscious stirs with hidden desire. The teacup and saucer, coded as feminine and domestic, are made wild and unsettling. Oppenheim was a serious, independent artist, not just Picasso's muse — and it's worth asking whether a male Surrealist would have even thought to subvert these particular objects.

**Transition from Dalí:** Dalí's doubling is an optical trick of the mind. Oppenheim's fur cup is a physical, bodily confrontation — you don't just see it, you feel it in your mouth.

- **Readymade lineage:** Duchamp invented the readymade — taking a manufactured object and declaring it art. Oppenheim extends this: she doesn't just select an object, she transforms it, turning the familiar into the viscerally alien
- **The collision:** cool porcelain (civility, ritual) meets warm fur (the body, the wild) — the Surrealist strategy of juxtaposing incompatible realities
- **Visceral reaction:** imagine drinking from it — wet fur, liquid seeping through. The disgust is the point
- **Freudian reading:** conscious mind says "repulsive," unconscious mind says "yes" — the gap between public self and private desire
- **Gender coding:** teacup and saucer = feminine propriety, domestic ritual. Oppenheim makes them brutal and animal
- **Agency matters:** the origin story (Picasso said "you could cover anything with fur," Oppenheim replied "even this cup") risks centering him. She was 23, already a serious artist
- **Ask students:** would a male Surrealist have used these objects? What does the choice of a teacup specifically bring to the work?
-->

### automatic drawing ✏️
</div>

<!--
André Masson fought in World War I and was severely wounded — the trauma haunted him for life and fueled the violent, primal imagery that defines his art. He joined the Surrealist movement at its founding in 1924, contributing automatic drawings to *La Révolution surréaliste*, though his anarchist temperament made him an uneasy member: he broke with Breton in 1929, rejoined in the 1930s, and left again in 1943. *Battle of Fishes* (1926) is his landmark sand painting — he spread adhesive directly on the canvas, sprinkled sand over it, then painted and drew into the textured surface, allowing gravity and chance to shape the composition. Two fish lock in primordial combat while surrounding forms suggest teeth, claws, and the raw violence of nature. This is automatism made physical: the hand moves without planning, the sand falls where it will, and the result reveals something the conscious mind would never invent.

**Transition from Oppenheim:** Oppenheim’s fur cup shocks the body through juxtaposition. Masson’s technique — automatic drawing and sand painting — shocks through process: surrendering control to chance and the unconscious, discovering images rather than designing them.

- **WWI trauma:** severely wounded in combat; the brutality of nature and human violence became his lifelong subject
- **Sand painting technique:** adhesive applied freely to canvas, sand sprinkled on, then oil, pencil, and charcoal worked into the textured surface — gravity and chance co-author the image
- **Automatism in practice:** not pure unconscious creation — Masson began automatically but then shaped and responded to what emerged, a dialogue between chance and intention
- **The imagery:** two fish in ferocious combat, surrounded by jagged teeth-like forms and aggressive charcoal marks — the brutality of all living things
- **Contrast with Dalí:** Dalí’s paranoiac-critical method is controlled, precise, calculated. Masson’s automatism is loose, urgent, improvisational — closer to jazz than to academic painting
- **Contributions to *La Révolution surréaliste*:** Masson’s automatic drawings were published regularly from 1925, making him one of the movement’s most visible practitioners of the technique
-->
