<!--
- Roger Fry (1866–1934), English artist and critic, coined “Post-Impressionism”
- He organized the First Post-Impressionist exhibition at the Grafton Galleries in London, Nov 1910 – Jan 1911 — this is the poster for that show
- The term described predominantly French artists influenced by Impressionism but wanting to move beyond it: Cézanne, Gauguin, Seurat, Van Gogh
- These artists had shown alongside the Impressionists at the Salon des Indépendants in Paris in 1886
- Blurrier edges: Gauguin and Redon are also classified as Symbolists due to their use of literary texts — yet Post-Impressionists are generally treated as heirs to Impressionism, contrasted with Symbolists who are seen as closer to the academy
- Core stylistic traits: color as an emotional/aesthetic carrier (almost independent of form); acceptance of the picture's artificiality; commitment to the portable easel painting as the highest artistic mode

<!--
- On the other side of the river from the Bathers is La Grande Jatte — Seurat’s most famous painting
- The crowning achievement of Neo-Impressionism: A Summer Sunday on the Island of the Grande Jatte (1884–86), shown at the final Impressionist exhibition of 1886
- The surface is built with discrete touches of paint, then a layer of painted dots — its sheer mechanical orderliness almost exhausts the viewer
- Based on prolonged study on the island, but actually constructed in a small Parisian studio from many oil studies and drawings — not plein air
- Its reference to the static, dynastic art of ancient Egypt collides intentionally with its modern, transitory Realist subject or a Panathenaic procession, the victory of the Athenians at Marathon
- Its large scale and visual ambition made it hard for other artists to work in its wake. Seurat treated each subsequent painting as a calculated move in an aesthetic chess game that ended with his early death
- Contrast with the Bathers: Grande Jatte depicts the middle and upper-middle class — fully clothed, formal, with social signals placing them in a higher echelon

Working-Class Subjects
- Clothing signals class: bowler hats, straw hats, informality — that’s how Seurat signals class status
- In preparatory sketches, a horse was shown drinking from the river — something that wouldn’t be allowed where the upper class bathed

<!--
- Seurat’s “The Bathers at Asnières” — his early large masterpiece. The figures are life-size or larger; it’s a big painting.
- Traditionally, artists painted bathers as timeless nudes. Here: real Parisian people at a specific place just outside Paris.
- Industrial backdrop: modern bridge, smokestacks, buildings. Not the picturesque countryside
- Seurat contrasts this group with higher-status figures in the rowboat at right — top hat, parasol, fully clothed despite the heat, with someone in shirtsleeves doing the rowing for them

Studio Painting, Not Plein Air
- This is a large studio painting, not painted outdoors — despite its casual subject
- The result feels more structured, more composed than Impressionism. Seurat is rejecting spontaneity in favor of rigor — almost a kind of classicism

Leisure Time as Subject
- Seurat draws attention to something new in the 19th century: the idea of scheduled leisure. You clocked in and out of those factories, then had separate leisure time — just like we do today

Technique: Proto-Pointillism
- Seurat is often called a pointillist, but this painting is a bit early for the dots. Instead you see small cross-hatchings — placing colors next to each other rather than mixing them on the palette, achieving a more complex color construction
-->

## Divisionism / Neo-Impressionism ✏️

:: content ::

![Left: Paul Signac, Evening Calm, Concarneau, Opus 220 (Allegro Maestoso), detail, 1891, oil on canvas, 64.9 x 81.3 cm (The Metropolitan Museum of Art); Right: The color wheel](https://smarthistory.org/wp-content/uploads/2020/04/Signac-color.jpg)

<Box v-drag="[508,185,415,162]" p-2 v-click>

> The Neo-Impressionist does not dot, he divides.

<div text-xs>

Paul Signac
</div>
</Box>

<!--
- First successfully independent art movement after Impressionism — called Neo-Impressionism or “Scientific Impressionism” by its practitioners
- Master of the movement: Georges Seurat, who died at just 32 in 1891, after painting a small number of self-conscious masterpieces
- Two strands combined: light theory + artificial pictorial construction
- As Signac said: “The Neo-Impressionist does not dot, he divides”
- influenced by the color theories of Michel Eugène Chevreul, Ogden Rood, and Charles Blanc — who argued that colors placed next to each other would mix optically in the viewer’s eye, creating a more vibrant effect than mixing pigments on the palette 
- Division achieves “luminosity” and “harmony” through four principles:
  1. Optical mixture of purely pure pigments (all prism tints and tones)
  2. Separation of elements (local color, lighting color, their interactions)
  3. Equilibration of elements and proportions (laws of contrast, gradation, irradiation)
  4. Choice of brushstroke commensurate with the painting's dimensions
-->

## Synthetism / Symbolism ✏️

:: content ::

<div h-110>

![Paul Gauguin, _Vision after the Sermon: Jacob Wrestling with the Angel_, 1888, oil on canvas](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/56/La_vision_apr%C3%A8s_le_sermon_%28Paul_Gauguin%29.jpg)
</div>

<!--
- Brittany (located on the north coast of France): a rugged region of France that attracted many artists in the late 19th century. Gauguin went there to separate himself from modern Parisian culture — to find something more true, more uncivilized, closer to original human nature
- The center of the movement: Pont-Aven (the "School of Pont-Aven") and the village of Le Pouldu, where Gauguin and followers worked in the second half of the 1880s
- Gauguin’s art developed from the Impressionism of his teacher Pissarro. The Synthetist movement was defined by texts written by Gauguin and others. They exhibited only once — in a cafe at the International Exhibition of 1889

Gauguin’s Break with Impressionism (1888)
- His split became absolute when he painted this work for the church in Pont-Aven. The church rejected it as an altarpiece — it’s been considered revolutionary ever since
- Synthetist principle: the artist must dominate rather than be submissive to nature

About the Painting
- the story: it is the biblical story of Jacob wrestling with the angel — a story of spiritual struggle and transformation. 
- It’s not a biblical subject. It’s not just Jacob wrestling with the angel. It’s a painting about people having a religious experience — the Breton women in their distinctive coiffes, experiencing a vision after a sermon
- The bold red field is not naturalistic — it’s the imaginative space where the vision unfolds. Reality and hallucination occupy the same picture plane
-->

### Does this painting follow the rules of perspective? 🗨️

![Paul Cézanne, _Still Life with Plaster Cupid_, c. 1894 oil on canvas, 70.6 x 57.3 cm](https://res.cloudinary.com/image-solar/image/upload/v1775061776/2026nwp-ar1205/P.1948.SC.59_c_sknrqi.jpg)

<!--
- Cézanne asks: which is more true — the objective or the subjective view? His conclusion mirrors an important development of modernism

The Subject: A Plaster Cupid
- A plaster cast (copy) of an ancient Roman cupid placed on a tabletop, dominating the composition
- For a 19th-century artist, classical sculpture = the humanist triumph of Greeks and Romans, the birth of naturalism
- The cupid is in contrapposto, but Cézanne exaggerates the twist — creating a spiral torsion that becomes an axis for the entire painting

Space Shaped by the Body
- The floor rises too steeply; stacked canvases form an uneven perimeter
- The canvases that line the floor shape the space of the room — and this shaping relates to the cupid’s twist:
  - Right foot points toward us → aligns with the table’s receding orthogonal
  - Hips turned → aligned with the canvas at the far left, partly hidden behind the blue tablecloth
  - Shoulders turned further → aligned with the canvas behind the cupid’s torso
- This is not objective architectural space. Cézanne matches the perception of space to the movement of the body
- Isn’t that what we really experience? We don’t see a room as an objective whole — we see continuously, and space is shaped by our movement through it. Cézanne renders true human vision: subjective, continuous, informed by memory

Looking Closely
- Everything is slightly shifting: the cupid twists, outlines of the fruit shift slightly, nothing is stable — everything in flux
- A giant piece of fruit (or ball?) on the floor pushes the canvas up and forward, denying spatial depth
- Alignments: the onion in the lower left — its skin ends where the floor line begins, continuing up to the cupid’s hip, then to the canvas edge
- Not that Cézanne didn’t know what he was doing — mixing viewpoints and twisting everything was a conscious choice. This is still carefully composed
- Cezanne took time to work the subject and each time the viewpoint shifted, the painting changed. Simultaneous views and shifting perspectives

Legacy
- This anticipates Matisse’s “Red Studio” — an artist’s studio with stacked canvases, unified by close-to-single tonality (blues with reds and greens)
- Meyer Shapiro reads the personal into Cézanne through the forms in the still life
- The modernists of the early 20th century picked up on this radical reassembling of space
- The central question: Do we construct space as we move through it? Is space subjective and constructed, rather than an ideal architectural given?
-->

![Paul Cézanne, _Mont Sainte-Victoire_, 1902–06. oil on canvas](https://res.cloudinary.com/image-solar/image/upload/v1775062370/2026nwp-ar1205/77J_045R2M_p24ri7.jpg)

<!--
- Cézanne is best known for two things: still lifes with apples, and landscapes of this mountain — Mont Sainte-Victoire in Provence
- He painted this mountain over and over, from many viewpoints, across decades. This version (1902–04, Philadelphia Museum of Art) is from the last years of his life — he died just two years after completing it
- Unlike Impressionism (rapid, on-site), Cézanne worked on each painting over an extended period. It feels unfinished: canvas shows through, buildings seem half-formed, the mountain itself is in process of forming

- This directly attacks the landscape tradition from Poussin and Claude: instead of believable space, Cézanne creates a "curtain of paint" where everything rises to the surface, announcing its two-dimensionality

The Composition
- Three roughly equal horizontal bands: foliage/houses in the foreground, yellow-green patchwork of the plain in the middle, blues and violets of mountain and sky above
- Subtle adjustments avoid a too-simple scheme: the peak is pushed right of center, the horizon inclines gently upward left to right, and a counterpoint of diagonals connects foreground to background

Flatness vs. Depth
- The central tension: deep recession that we expect from landscape vs. radical confrontation with the flat surface
- He evokes atmosphere that fills and unifies the space, but we remain acutely aware of the painting as a rough, deftly worked surface
- Flatness coexists with depth — we're caught between these two poles
-->

<!--
- Mont Sainte-Victoire is a limestone peak at 3,317 ft (1,011m), overlooking Aix-en-Provence (pronounced "aks-ahn-provans") — Cézanne's hometown. Not a giant mountain, but it commands the landscape
- Cézanne built a studio on the hill of Les Lauves in 1901–02. From there he walked uphill to this viewpoint. Emile Bernard recalled: "Cézanne picked up a box in the hall and took me to his motif... the craggy mountain which he never ceased to paint"

- Everything is built from hash marks that create optical movement — look too closely and the forms fall apart

Compare Painting vs. Photo
- In the painting, Cézanne treats every part of the canvas the same way — there's no atmospheric perspective (no fading or loss of clarity in the distance, which is what we'd expect)
- Instead, distance is shown purely through color choice: blue-browns in the foreground, reds and greens in the middle, blues in the distance — an arbitrary association of place with color
- He brings color from one zone into another: gray-purple from the foreground appears in the sky, making the sky come forward
- The mountain has a double peak in reality — a higher point on the left, lower on the right. In the painting this is hard to read: a patch of bare canvas (the priming) near the top can be read as sky or cloud, and the brushstrokes below it describe the actual downward slant

A note on distance: from Les Lauves to the summit is about 10 miles (16 km) as the crow flies — the mountain dominates the view but is actually quite far away

<Box v-drag="[125,177,736,185]" p-2 v-click>

> I am plowing on my canvases as they do in their fields.

<span text-xs> Vincent van Gogh, Letter to Theo van Gogh</span>
</Box>

<!--
- This is not the Van Gogh most people expect — no bright colors, no landscape. Dark, muted, a figure painting. Painted in 1885, over a decade after the first Impressionist exhibition, so this darkness is a conscious choice.
- Van Gogh worked through many 19th-century styles — Millet, the Impressionists, the Neo-Impressionists (Seurat) — before arriving at a direct application of paint that felt authentic to him.

The Subject: Peasant Life
- He’s not following Impressionism here. He’s linking himself to a tradition of peasant painting — Dutch artists like Israëls, French painters like Millet
- Five figures around a table. A woman pours coffee; they’re about to eat potatoes — likely all they have
- The dark browns deliberately echo the meager life of peasants bound to the earth — the food they eat is what they grew themselves

The Style: Intentional “Mistakes”
- The space, perspective, and anatomy are “not quite right” — the background doesn’t make sense, figures have awkward anatomy (shoulders not correctly attached)
- Look at the hand of the man holding the cup — the cup doesn’t sit in the palm properly
- This is early Van Gogh, still struggling with perspective and the human body — but his primary goal wasn’t technical perfection. He wanted an authentic relationship between the rough paint and the rough subjects

Meaning & Sincerity
- A painting about sincerity — sincere about peasant life, sincere to himself
- Van Gogh came from a religious background (father was a parson; he tried to be a minister). He saw peasant life as more spiritually truthful
- Layers and layers of paint — he worked the canvas heavily. Quote: “I am plowing on my canvases as they do in their fields.”
- He is willing himself into a simpler, more spiritually authentic world

Van Gogh’s Personal Connection
- His ambition was to be a peasant painter — to be a peasant. This horrified his middle-class family
- Correcting the myth: he came from a prosperous background (uncle was a wealthy print dealer). He was always an outsider
- In this painting he creates a closely-knit family environment — a place where he belongs. Much of his life was spent trying to recreate the family from which he felt estranged

Context in Art History
- This interest in peasant life aligns him with Gauguin — shared desire to leave the city for something authentic
- Also allied with Courbet, who rejected the polish of the capital to paint his rural hometown
- Theo offered to submit a painting to the Paris Salon for him. Van Gogh sent this. Imagine Theo opening the box — Salon paintings were polished, with correct space and anatomy. This was the opposite.

In Van Gogh’s own words (letter to Theo):
“It would be wrong to give a painting of peasant life a conventional polish. If a peasant painting smells of bacon, smoke, potato steam, fine — that’s not unhealthy. If a stable reeks of manure — all right, that’s what a stable is all about... a painting of peasant life should not be perfumed.”
- This painting is not perfumed. It’s about art having moral agency — part of the 19th-century avant-garde tradition
-->

- The Bedroom — painted in the Yellow House in Arles, where Van Gogh moved from Paris. He painted three versions of this.
- The space tilts and rushes backward; things seem slightly askew. The perspective doesn’t quite make sense. But his brushwork in the pillows almost describes their puffiness physically.

A Refuge and a Utopian Vision
- Van Gogh wanted this to be the basis for a community of artists — a place to focus on making art, away from the materialism of Paris
- This is a deeply personal refuge. He created the space with love and care, inviting us to feel at home — conveying his intimate experience of having touched that seat, having slept in that bed

The Quote — Root of Abstraction
- The on-screen quote is from a letter to Theo, referring to the first version of the painting

Connection to Broader Themes
- Authenticity is the key word for many artists at this moment, including Gauguin — finding authentic experience outside the city

![Vincent van Gogh, The Starry Night, 1889, oil on canvas, 73.7 x 92.1 cm](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Van_Gogh_-_Starry_Night_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/3840px-Van_Gogh_-_Starry_Night_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg)

<!--
- Painted late June/early July 1889, while Van Gogh was at the asylum of Saint-Paul-de-Mausole in Saint-Rémy — after his breakdown and the ear incident with Gauguin
- The sky takes up almost three-quarters of the canvas. Only light: the moon and stars, pulsing with energy far brighter than reality

The Scene
- A giant cypress tree in the foreground — cut off at the bottom, so it feels close to us. Traditionally a symbol of death (found in cemeteries), but its flame-like form links the earthly and the heavenly
- Below: a valley with humble cottages and a church centrally placed, its steeple breaking the horizon. The steeple mimics the cypress — both reaching upward
- A band of lighter yellows and blues above the hills seems to protect the village. Turmoil in the sky contrasts with the tranquility of the village below
- Complementary colors: blues and yellows, oranges — intensifying one another to communicate ideas and feelings

Not What He Saw
- This is not a landscape Van Gogh observed — it's constructed from memory, imagination, and elements from earlier works. Van Gogh called it simply a "study of night" or "night effect"
- His brother Theo was unimpressed: "I feel that the search for style takes away the real sentiment of things"
- This is brave for an artist who almost always painted what he could see. Van Gogh himself called the abstraction of form here "absolutely radical"
-->

<!--
Two Approaches to the Night Sky
- Right: Starry Night over the Rhône (1888) — painted outdoors in Arles, with the help of gas lamplight. Plein air. Shown at the Salon des Indépendants in 1889
- Left: The Starry Night (1889) — painted in a studio at the asylum with no view of the landscape. Composed from previous works, memory, and imagination
- Van Gogh had been thinking about painting a starry sky for months. He wrote to Emile Bernard in 1888: "A starry sky... it's a thing that I'd like to try to do, but how to arrive at that unless I decide to work at home and from the imagination?"

What's Invented
- There is no church or village visible from Van Gogh's window at Saint-Rémy. The church spire is likely more Dutch in character — an amalgamation of spires he painted years earlier in the Netherlands
- Van Gogh was experimenting with a style inspired by medieval woodcuts: thick outlines, simplified forms. He told Theo: "These are exaggerations from the point of view of arrangement, their lines are contorted like those of ancient woodcuts"

Observation Behind the Invention
- Van Gogh spent hours looking at the night sky in rural southern France (no city gas or electric lights). He wrote to his sister Willemien: "It often seems to me that the night is even more richly colored than the day, colored with the most intense violets, blues and greens. If you look carefully, you'll see that some stars are lemony, others have a pink, green, forget-me-not blue glow"
- The painting demonstrates the wide variety of colors he actually perceived on clear nights — but arranged and exaggerated
-->

- Painted in 1897 during Gauguin's second trip to Tahiti. Classic Gauguin — but more complex than the myth suggests
- Gauguin went to Tahiti expecting an untouched civilization, free from modern society. What he found was much more developed and touristic. He painted the fantasy anyway

A Traditional Nude, But Different
- In many ways this is a traditional French academic nude: full-length, reclining, stretched across the canvas. But it's not a Venus
- Unlike academic nudes (passive, laid out for the viewer's pleasure), this woman has independent presence: a sidelong glance, her right arm protecting her chest, her left hand drawing attention up to her face
- She's thinking — we can see her thinking, reacting. The bright yellow pillow behind her head draws our eye there more than to her body

Decorative Flattening and Dreamspace
- The curves of the headboard, the curve of her back, the arabesques on the wallpaper — flattening decorative forms remove the figure from the everyday and place her in a dreamlike space
- The bright citron yellow of her pillow appears again only in the clouds. The red at her feet appears in the trees outside. The landscape through the window/doorway feels like an illustration for a children's book — a fantasy, a dream
- Perhaps we're not looking at a real scene but at her dream, her imaginings

The Raven
- The title "Nevermore" refers to Edgar Allan Poe's poem. The raven is rendered with the same flattened, decorative quality as the wallpaper — almost part of the patterning
- Something psychological and emotive here: pulling away surface appearances to evoke an internal emotional state — specifically her emotional state

<!--
- Oviri (1894) — arguably Gauguin's masterpiece in ceramics, made during a tempestuous period between his two Tahiti trips
- Gauguin returned from his first Tahiti stay in 1893 to no fanfare, no sales, and his wife Mette cutting all ties. A drunken brawl with Breton sailors shattered his ankle. In June 1895 he left for Tahiti for good. Oviri was made in this crisis period
- The largest and last of his ceramic works, made with help from ceramist Ernest Chaplet in Paris. Clay was cheap, and Gauguin hoped "art pottery" would attract more buyers than his paintings (it didn't)
- He loved non-European art — Inca and pre-Columbian pottery from his Peruvian childhood. For him, decorative/utilitarian objects could express profound emotion as much as "high art"

The Right Image: Where Are You Going? (1892)
- Painted during his first Tahiti stay. The stocky, sculptural, androgynous foreground figure carrying a wolf-cub is the earliest design seed for Oviri
- Compare: the painting is gentle; the ceramic is a far more radical departure — an "atavistic assault on the canons of grace, harmony and beauty"

Oviri: The Savage
- Oviri-moe-aihere = "wild-one-who-sleeps-in- the-wilderness" — the Tahitian goddess of death and mourning. Gauguin used the name as a nom de guerre for himself, taking on the goddess's terrible aspects
- Under her feet: a dead she-wolf. In her hands: its crushed cub. Red glaze suggests trickling blood. No known source for the episode — probably Gauguin's invention
- Interpretations: the wolves = savagery of the goddess; she absorbs their violence; they are Gauguin himself (Degas called him "the hungry wolf without a collar"); a veiled reference to infanticide by Tahiti's Areoi priestly elite; a vengeful mother archetype connecting to Delacroix's Medea About to Kill Her Children (1838)
- The block-like frontal form (like Egyptian statuary) commands one view. From the back: hair becomes a cocoon-like covering, a chrysalis on the point of breaking out — death and life confounded
- Striking likeness to Rodin's Balzac (in progress since 1891): same dramatic reduction of form, rejection of classical reference, tapping into primordial impulse

Legacy
- In 1900, knowing he had not long to live, Gauguin asked a friend to send Oviri from Paris for his tombstone. The friend never did
- It appeared in Gauguin's 1906 retrospective — which Picasso saw and most likely drew inspiration from for Les Demoiselles d'Avignon
- A bronze cast now stands at Gauguin's grave in the Marquesas; the original is at the Musée d'Orsay
