<!-- 

from some sort of realism to Post-impressionism: here’s an artist who has really worked through a catalog of the styles of the 19th century. Beginning, for instance, with the art of people like Millet, moving through the Impressionists, and then really paying attention to the Neo-Impressionists, people like Seurat, [and] here, finding a a direct application of paint that, for Van Gogh felt absolutely authentic.


Hook: This painting is different from what we normally expect from Van Gogh.

    Instead of a bright landscape, we have a dark figure painting.

    The colors are intentionally muted, not bright.

Setting the Date: Painted in 1885.

    This is over a decade after the first Impressionist exhibition.

    Brilliant colors were already well-established, so this darkness is a conscious choice.

1. The Subject: Peasant Life

Art Historical Roots: He’s not following Impressionism here. Instead, he’s linking himself to a tradition of peasant painting.

    Think of earlier Dutch artists (like Israëls) and French painters (like Millet).

The Scene: Five figures gathered around a table.

    A woman is pouring coffee; they are about to eat potatoes.

    This simple meal is likely all they have.

Connection to the Earth:

    The dark colors (browns) are a deliberate link to the meager life of the peasants.

    They are bound to the earth. The food they eat is likely a bounty they grew themselves, highlighting the relationship between their labor and their sustenance.

3. The Style: Intentional "Mistakes"

"Imperfect" Technique: The space, perspective, and anatomy are "not quite right."

    The background space doesn't make complete sense.

    Figures have awkward anatomy (e.g., shoulders don't seem correctly attached).

    Specific Detail: Look at the hand of the man holding the cup—the relationship between the cup and the hand is problematic; it doesn't sit in the palm.

A Struggling Artist? It looks like Van Gogh doesn’t know how to paint, and that’s because this is very early in his career. He is still struggling with perspective, foreshortening, and the human body.

The Point: His primary goal wasn't technical perfection.

    He wanted to create an authentic relationship between the rough application of paint and the rough quality of his subjects.

4. Meaning & Sincerity

Sincerity: This is a painting about sincerity—sincere about peasant life, and sincere to himself.

Spiritual Truth:

    Van Gogh came from a religious background (his father was a parson; he had tried to be a minister).

    He saw peasant life not just as authentic, but as more spiritually truthful and connected to a deeper human condition.

Creating the Painting:

    There are layers and layers of paint. He worked the painting heavily.

    Quote from Van Gogh: “I am plowing on my canvases as they do in their fields.”

    He is willing himself into what he saw as a simpler, more spiritually authentic world.

5. Van Gogh’s Personal Connection

Aspiring Downward: His ambition was to be a peasant painter—to be a peasant.

    This horrified his middle-class/upper-middle-class family. They expected him to aspire upward.

Correcting the Myth:

    The myth says he was poor, but he came from a prosperous background (his uncle was a wealthy print dealer).

    He was always an outsider, an unstable personality who struggled to cope with the world.

A Search for Belonging:

    In this painting, he creates a contained, closely-knit family environment—a place where he belongs.

    Much of his life was spent trying to recreate the family from which he felt estranged.

6. Context in Art History

Post-Impressionism & Gauguin:

    This interest in peasant life aligns him with other Post-Impressionists like Gauguin.

    The shared desire was to leave behind the city, its fashions, and its artificiality for something authentic.

Connection to Courbet:

    He is more allied with earlier artists like Courbet, who rejected the polish and elitism of the capital city to paint his rural hometown.

Opposition to the Salon:

    His brother, Theo, offered to submit a painting to the prestigious Paris Salon for him. Van Gogh sent this.

    Imagine Theo’s face opening the box. Salon paintings were polished, with correct space and anatomy. This painting was the opposite of what was expected.

7. Conclusion: The Artist’s Own Words

Van Gogh’s Philosophy:

    Quote from a letter to Theo:

        “It would be wrong to give a painting of peasant life a conventional polish. If a peasant painting smells of bacon, smoke, potato steam, fine — that’s not unhealthy. If a stable reeks of manure — all right, that’s what a stable is all about. If a field has the smell of ripe corn or potatoes or of guano or manure — that’s properly healthy, especially for city dwellers. Such pictures might prove helpful to them, but a painting of peasant life should not be perfumed.”

Final Thought:

    This painting is not perfumed. It’s about art having moral agency—righting the wrongs of the industrial, modern world—and being part of the 19th-century avant-garde tradition.

Brittany was a region in France that attracted many artists in the late 19th century, including Paul Gauguin, who was a leading figure in the Synthetist movement. an area that’s rugged and difficult weather. To achieve an aesthetic synthesis between artist and subject, the Synthetist artist must dominate rather than be submissiveto nature.
