## _Fauvism_

<!--
- Fauvism means "the wild ones," a label critics used for intense, even "savage," color around 1905-1906.
- Core artists include Henri Matisse, Andre Derain, Raoul Dufy, and Maurice de Vlaminck.

- Link it to Neo-Impressionism: after Seurat, Signac and Cross moved from tiny dots to broader patches and dabs.
- That shift made color less about optical science and more about building the picture surface.
- Fauvism also develops Post-Impressionist breakthroughs from Gauguin and van Gogh: color can carry emotion, not just describe nature.
- Timeline cue for class: strongest push toward fully autonomous color arrives around 1907-1908.

- Before World War I, Fauvism spread internationally through exhibitions, shaping artists from Australia to Central Europe.
- Key takeaway for students: in Fauvism, color is structural and expressive, independent of literal appearance.
-->

## _Cubism_

---

![Pablo Picasso, _The Guitarist_, 1910. Oil on canvas](https://res.cloudinary.com/image-solar/image/upload/v1775028612/2026nwp-ar2205/www.artchive.com_artwork_the-guitar-player-pablo-picasso-1910__vicfkt.png)

<!--
- Introduce Analytic Cubism as "taking apart" form rather than describing appearance.
- Tell students to step back mentally: the guitarist emerges only after sustained looking.
- Stress that Picasso is not failing at realism; he is rejecting Renaissance single-point vision.
- Connect to modernity: fractured experience, unstable perception, multiple viewpoints over time.
- Draw attention to the restrained palette: browns, grays, blacks keep focus on structure.
- Clarify that space is shallow, not absent; volume survives as fragments and overlaps.
- End with the key takeaway: Cubism proposes a different truth, not less truth.
-->

### Suprematism

---

![Kazimir Malevich, Suprematist Composition: White on White, 1918, oil on canvas, 79.4 x 79.4 cm](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4c/White_on_White_%28Malevich%2C_1918%29.png)

<!--
- Define Suprematism as a search for pure feeling through non-objective form.
- Historicize 1918: revolutionary optimism made abstraction feel politically and spiritually urgent.
- Direct attention to subtle differences: warm outer white, cool inner white, slight tilt.
- Explain the paradox: minimal image, but unstable reading (flat plane, depth cue, possible motion).
- Connect to modernist rejection of bourgeois naturalism and inherited iconography.
- End with trajectory: avant-garde utopia narrowed under Stalinist demands for Socialist Realism.
-->
