## _Fauvism_

<!--
- Fauvism means "the wild ones," a label critics used for intense, even "savage," color around 1905-1906. Louis Vauxcelles coined the term after seeing Matisse's work at the Salon d'Automne in 1905, where he famously described the paintings as "Donatello among the wild beasts."
  
- Core artists include Henri Matisse, Andre Derain, Raoul Dufy, and Maurice de Vlaminck.

- Link it to Neo-Impressionism: after Seurat, Signac and Cross moved from tiny dots to broader patches and dabs.
- That shift made color less about optical science and more about building the picture surface.
- Fauvism also develops Post-Impressionist breakthroughs from Gauguin and van Gogh: color can carry emotion, not just describe nature.
- Timeline cue for class: strongest push toward fully autonomous color arrives around 1907-1908.

- Before World War I, Fauvism spread internationally through exhibitions, shaping artists from Australia to Central Europe.
- Key takeaway for students: in Fauvism, color is structural and expressive, independent of literal appearance.
-->

## Expressionism

<!-- 

- Unlike France, Germany had no single cultural capital — its modern art was regional and decentralized.
- Precursors: Max Liebermann and Lovis Corinth brought raw, emotionally charged painting that made French work feel precise and cool by comparison.

- Two key avant-garde groups define German Expressionism:
  - Die Brücke (founded 1905, Dresden): led by Ernst Kirchner.
  - Der Blaue Reiter (founded 1911, Munich): Franz Marc, Gabriele Münter, and Wassily Kandinsky.

- Unlike Parisian modernists, they drew inspiration from Folk art
- Like the Fauves, Expressionists used high-intensity color rooted in van Gogh and Gauguin.
- Key difference: for Expressionists, emotional power of the subject mattered as much as the color.
- Subjects range widely: social class, commodified sexuality, animals, mythology, and folktales.
- In many ways, Expressionists fused Realism and Symbolism with Fauvist chromatic excess.

- Note for students: German Expressionism is often underplayed in Anglo-American surveys due to historical anti-German bias.
- Yet its European reach was extraordinary, especially in Russia and Central Europe, where Germanic culture rivaled French influence.
 -->

## _Cubism_

<!--
- First artistic movement to rival Impressionism in global reach and significance.
- Named by critic Vauxcelles in 1908, who mocked Braque's paintings as "bizarreries cubiques."
- Rooted in Cézanne: the massive 1907 retrospective in Paris set the stage for young artists.

- Cubism breaks down the object and rejects expressive color — a radical strategy on par with the invention of one-point perspective in the Renaissance.
- Apologists saw it as a complete rebirth of painting, redefining the relationship between seeing and representing.

- Two canonical practitioners: Pablo Picasso and Georges Braque, careers so intertwined (1907-14) their work is hard to tell apart.
- Theory came from the Puteaux Cubists (Gleizes, Metzinger), not from Picasso or Braque themselves — theory postdated the practice.

- Key followers: Juan Gris, Fernand Léger, Robert Delaunay.
- Broader circle: Marcel Duchamp, Henri le Fauconnier, Roger de la Fresnaye, and others.
- Even artists who rejected Cubism — Mondrian, Severini, Malevich — had to grapple with it first.
- Far more international than French-centered histories suggest: major practitioners in New York, Milan, Prague, Bucharest, and Moscow by 1909-10.
-->

- Clarify that space is shallow, not absent; volume survives as fragments and overlaps.
- End with the key takeaway: Cubism proposes a different truth, not less truth.

### Analytic Cubism ✏️

![Pablo Picasso, _Girl with a Mandolin (Fanny Tellier)_, 1910. Oil on canvas, 100.3 x 73.6 cm,](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/1/1c/Pablo_Picasso%2C_1910%2C_Girl_with_a_Mandolin_%28Fanny_Tellier%29%2C_oil_on_canvas%2C_100.3_x_73.6_cm%2C_Museum_of_Modern_Art_New_York..jpg)

<!--
- Cubism has two successive phases (unlike Realism/Impressionism/Symbolism, which developed simultaneously): Analytic, then Synthetic.
- Analytic phase (exemplified by Picasso's Portrait of Ambroise Vollard, 1909-10): monochromatic, shifting geometric image that fills the center and radiates outward.
- Analytic method: the artist studies the subject over time, analyzing its pictorial character — short black lines and patches of paint in clustered strokes to wrest contour and value from the subject.
- The goal: assemble "essential" qualities of the pictured subject on the canvas surface.

- Synthetic phase parallels the shift from Impressionism's optical description to Synthetism.
- Rooted in collage: images built not only with painted/drawn marks but with pasted elements from popular visual culture — wallpaper, sheet music, posters, newspapers, theatre tickets.
- The image is a synthesis of hand-made and borrowed elements — making clear that representation is artificial and essentially flat.
- Collage forges aesthetic and political links between Cubists and early Realists: the artist as urban image-maker working with commercial materials.

- Contrast the two phases quickly: Analytic Cubism breaks forms down; Synthetic Cubism builds images up.
- Mention "LE JOU" as both newspaper fragment and wordplay (journal, play, toy, day).
- Use the Balkan War headline to show how politics enters the still-life surface.
- Point out visual puns: shapes can read as guitar parts or as abstract signs.
- Conclude with method: Synthetic Cubism invites layered reading, not one fixed interpretation.
-->

## Futurism

<!--
- Italian modern art is often downplayed in surveys, but important precedents exist: the Macchiaioli (1850s-60s) and Divisionist painters of Milan (Pelizza, Morbelli, Segantini) in the 1890s.
- First powerful interaction between Italian and French avant-gardes came in the 1910s with the Futurists.

- Key figures:  Gino Severini, Umberto Boccioni, Giacomo Balla, Carlo Carrà, and Filippo Marinetti — moved fluidly between northern Italy and Paris, absorbing Cubist developments.
- Unlike the Cubists, Futurists were obsessed with words: manifestos, essays, and books advancing their aesthetic theories.

- Like the Impressionists, Futurists celebrated motion and the simultaneity of unrelated events.
- Combined Impressionist preoccupation with machines, railroads, and industrial transformation with Cubist pictorial inventions.
- Result: a synthetic urban art that is highly rhythmic and infused with the energy of rapidly moving time.

- Key distinction from Cubism: Cubist time = an observer studying a static subject. Futurist time = collective experience, not dependent on a single observer.
- The Cubists were visually unprepared for WWI. For the Futurists, war was not only inevitable but representable.
-->

![Constantin Brancusi, The Kiss, 1916, limestone, 58.4 x 33.7 x 25.4 cm](https://cdn.thecollector.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/09/constantin-brancusi-kiss-modern-sculpture-1916.jpg?width=599&quality=100&dpr=2)


<!--
SNIPPET: Brancusi strips sculpture down to a single limestone block — two figures separated by one incised line, arms locked around each other, mouths merged, eyes joining into a single cyclopean form. It looks simple, even childlike, but that directness is the point: truth found not in polished finish, but in the raw material itself.

TRANSITION: From the mechanized horse, we move to the opposite pole of modernism — not the machine, but the archaic. Brancusi turns away from academic sophistication toward something older and more primal.

VISUAL
- The block remains visible; form emerges from the stone instead of erasing it. Elbows align with the corners of the block; the cubic shape is preserved, not concealed.
- A single incised line separates the two figures — without it, they could almost read as one body. The line on the right curves to suggest breasts; her eye is slightly smaller, her frame slightly thinner.
- Hands clasp each other tightly across the divide. The mouths merge into one. The eyes meet at the center, forming a single shared eye.
- Surface is left rough, especially the hair — no academic polish, no sheen. Brancusi honors the limestone's own texture and structure.
- This is the fourth version of The Kiss — each iteration pushed further toward simplification and geometric unity.

HISTORICAL
- Brancusi worked briefly in Rodin's studio but rejected Rodin's approach. Where Rodin fragmented the figure and explored muscular movement, Brancusi sought the internal spirit and essential structure of form.
- He came to Paris as an outsider — a Romanian with deep roots in peasant traditions of stone and wood carving. He brought folk art's directness into the capital of academic art.
- This version (1916) was commissioned by an American collector who wanted the first version; Brancusi said it wasn't available and carved a new one.
- Brancusi didn't want the sculpture on a pedestal — he said it would be "a kind of amputation." He wanted it directly on the ground, removing sculpture from the elevated realm of the academy into lived space.
- His influence reached forward: Isamu Noguchi worked with Brancusi and carried forward his regard for material truth and internal form.

CULTURAL
- This is a radical rejection of 300–400 years of sculptural tradition — from Renaissance finish through Bernini's high-Baroque polish to 19th-century academic technique.
- Brancusi's "primitive" and archaic style connects to a broader modernist turn: avant-garde artists across Europe looked outside Paris and outside classical tradition for more authentic, "truthful" forms — African art, folk art, archaic Greek sculpture.
- The union of male and female into a single block carries symbolic weight: something elemental about the human condition, rendered in the most reduced language possible.
- The avant-garde, as Harris notes, is defined by rejecting academic authority and finding an alternative that speaks more genuinely to the artist's own time.
-->

### Suprematism

<!--
- Suprematism and its successor Constructivism are inseparable from the Russian Revolution — the most powerful movement in the creation of fully abstract art.
- Malevich claimed to have developed completely abstract art by 1913, but it was after the 1917 Revolution that his art became an exemplar of both pure aesthetic research and a new social order.

- First time in European art history: the most advanced art was directly linked to equally advanced social and political theory.
- Artists collaborated with architects, writers, musicians, dancers, and actors to transform the visual world — no more important social/aesthetic/political experiment in modern art history.
- Crushing defeat under Stalin: one of the gravest acts of censorship in modern history, only partially redeemed today.

- For Malevich, human consciousness — not the visual world — was the true (supreme) subject of art.**Define Suprematism as a search for pure feeling through non-objective form**.
- Radical leap: art composed of elements with no relationship to the forms of nature.
- Influences: Futurists (Malevich adopted the name by 1913, Marinetti visited St. Petersburg in 1914) and Russia's strong Symbolist tradition of expressing emotion through abstract elements.

- Russia had the artistic population to create revolutionary consciousness through images — the "Great Utopia" involved artists too numerous to list.
- Unlike other avant-gardes, this movement became part of the larger society it helped define.
- Its ultimate failure and marginalization makes it the only successful vanguard movement in the history of art to have been suppressed by the very society it shaped.
-->
