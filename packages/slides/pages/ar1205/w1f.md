Until roughly the fourteenth century, artists were typically grouped among the ‚Äúmechanical arts.‚Äù This broad field included professions ranging from agriculture to architecture and was distinguished from the more lofty field of the ‚Äúliberal arts.‚Äù While the liberal arts were valued higher because they stressed one‚Äôs intellectual abilities, the mechanical arts garnered less esteem because they were generally considered manual labor. Pressures to elevate the artistic profession, however, began in earnest in the midst of the rising Renaissance era. <!--

Alhazen 

Diagrams illustrating the structure of the eye were widespread in antiquity and the Middle Ages, commonly found in manuscripts and papyri. These visual aids helped explain complex physiological and optical theories about vision. The two dominant theories were intromission, which posited that light entered the eye and was processed into stimuli, and **extramission**, which suggested that rays from the eye gathered information from objects.

After Euclid and Ptolemy, a key contribution to optics came from the Islamic Golden Age through Abu Ali al-Hasan ibn al-Haytham (965‚Äì1040), known in the West as Alhazen.

Ibn al-Haytham‚Äôs experiments, detailed in "Kitab al-Manazir" (Latin: "De aspectibus"), supported the intromission theory by showing that light travels into the eye, refuting the earlier **extramission** view.

This diagram, from Ibn al-Haytham's Kitab al-Manazir (Book of Optics), details the structure of the human visual system (wrote between 1011 and 1021). It portrays the eyes and their connection to the brain through optical pathways. The circular structures represent the eyes, with intricate lines illustrating the journey of light rays as they enter the eyes, refract, and are processed to form visual images. The Arabic inscriptions describe various anatomical components, such as the eye's lens, optic nerve, and other parts involved in vision. This diagram exemplifies Ibn al-Haytham‚Äôs blend of geometric optics and anatomical insight.

### Ibn al-Haytham‚Äôs Camera Obscura

This illustration shows how light shines through a small hole in the darkened room, projecting an inverted image on the opposite sheet. He called this ‚ÄúQamara‚Äù, the world‚Äôs first Camera Obscura.

The defining civic project of the early years of the fifteenth century was the completion of Florence Cathedral with a magnificent dome over the high altar. The construction of the cathedral had begun in the late thirteenth century and had continued intermittently during the fourteenth. As early as 1367, builders had envisioned a very tall dome to span the huge interior space of the crossing, but they lacked the engineering know-how to construct it. When interest in completing the cathedral revived around 1407, the technical solution was proposed by the young sculp- tor-turned-architect Filippo Brunelleschi.

Brunellschi had studied the ruins of ancient Roman buildings, including the Pantheon with its massive unreinforced concrete dome. He understood that to build a dome of such size required a different approach than the traditional method of centering, which involved constructing a wooden framework to support the masonry during construction. Brunelleschi‚Äôs design for the dome was revolutionary.
