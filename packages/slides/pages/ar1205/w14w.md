Dr. Beth Harris: [0:02] We’re in the Wadsworth Atheneum, looking at a series of photographs by Mierle Laderman Ukeles. This is her work “Washing/Tracks/Maintenance: Outside,” from 1973. What we’re looking at is the artist scrubbing the outside staircase of this museum.

Patricia Hickson: [0:20] Mierle came here in the summer of 1973 and performed four different performance art pieces, each of them associated with the authority and power within [an] art museum and also about domestic maintenance.

Dr. Harris: [0:35] In these photographs, we see the artist going outside of the museum.

Patricia: [0:40] This is where people enter the museum. So she’s front and center at this institution, where everyone who visits the museum has to pass. [1:00] In a way, it’s like being an action painter like Jackson Pollock, but her palette is water and her surface is the concrete sidewalk and the stairs in front of the museum.

Dr. Harris: [1:09] I read that this took hours. This is backbreaking work.

Patricia: [1:13] I believe it was a four-hour performance, so that’s no small undertaking.

Patricia: [1:58] Mierle had a crisis of identity as an artist at that time and became very angry about her position as a mother having to be completely responsible for the care and maintenance of children who were completely dependent on her.

Dr. Harris: [2:14] She said when she was in her studio, she thought about whether the child was being taken care of. When she was with her children, she thought about her art. Was there a way to make caring for something, maintaining something, art?

Patricia: [2:26] She created a manifesto and a whole new area of art making, which she called “maintenance art.”

Dr. Harris: [2:33] If you think about this in the context of 20th century art, going back to Duchamp and the idea of the ready-made, of taking something that’s already out in the world and naming it art and by naming it art, transforming it, changing how we look at something, and Ukeles calls our attention to maintenance.

Patricia: [2:52] She approached a number of different museums about performing these tasks as performance art and was turned down by virtually every museum except for the Wadsworth Atheneum. Dr. Harris: [3:14] You used the term “institutional critique.” This is a way we think about some art which criticized the very institutions of art. The museum was opening itself up, potentially, to some criticism.

Patricia: [3:26] She’s shedding light on the invisible workers at an art museum that perhaps we all take for granted and are the workers who are not paid as well. They’re not on the front line, like a director or curator would be, but people who are maintenance staff invisibly keep the museum in perfect working order.

[3:45] What she’s doing as a performance piece during the hours that the museum is open is to expose a worker like this in a way that would highlight their actions.

Dr. Harris: [3:55] As a mother, there’s a maintenance system that’s within the family. And then here we have the maintenance system of a public institution like the Wadsworth Atheneum, a museum. Then later on in her work, she also looks at the maintenance system of a city.

Patricia: [4:10] We can relate to this on so many different levels, because in our personal lives we have to maintain our own homes and laundry. ![Judy Chicago, The Dinner Party, 1974–79, ceramic, porcelain, and textile, 1463 x 1463 cm](https://smarthistory.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/54454124641_6af0c46eb2_6k.jpg)

<!-- 

Her experience with Womanhouse inspired her to embrace materials that had traditionally been associated with women’s crafts, such as embroidery, weaving, and china painting. She was determined to make a monument to women’s history using china-painted plates alluding to thirteen specific figures, which she originally planned to hang on the gallery wall.

The Dinner Party is a monument to women’s history and accomplishments. It is a massive triangular table—measuring 48 feet on each side—with 39 place settings dedicated to prominent women throughout history and an additional 999 names are inscribed on the table’s glazed porcelain brick base. This tribute to women, which includes individual place settings for such luminary figures as the Primordial Goddess, Ishtar, Hatshepsut, Theodora, Artemesia Gentileschi, Sacajawea, Sojourner Truth, Susan B. Anthony, Elizabeth Cady Stanton, Emily Dickinson, Margaret Sanger, and Georgia O’Keeffe, is beautifully crafted. Each place setting has an exquisitely embroidered table runner that includes the name of the woman, utensils, a goblet, and a plate.

The Dinner Party was intended to be exhibited in a large, darkened, sanctuary-like room, with each place setting individually lit, making it look as though it is composed of thirty-nine altars. The 999 names, written in gold, gleam softly, suggesting a hallowed or liminal space. 5 years in the making (1974–79) and the product of the volunteer labor of more than 400 people, The Dinner Party is a testament to the power of feminist vision and artistic collaboration. It was also a testament to Chicago’s ability to create a work of art that spoke to people who had not previously been a part of the art world.

Nancy Holt is known for artworks that engage the celestial: stars, sunlight, and orbiting bodies. In 2018, with support from the Holt/Smithson Foundation, Dia acquired Holt’s “Sun Tunnels” (1973–76) and continues to steward the work to this day.

Composed of four concrete cylinders—18 feet in length and 9 feet in diameter—“Sun Tunnels” is located in the Great Basin Desert in northwestern Utah. The work centers Holt’s interest in perception and time, sculpting the sun’s light through the interplay of land and sky and shifts from day to night.

- Holt’s “Sun Tunnels” is a land art installation located in the Great Basin Desert in Utah. The work consists of four large concrete tunnels arranged in an X shape, with each tunnel measuring 18 feet in length and 9 feet in diameter. The tunnels are positioned to align with the sunrise and sunset during the summer and winter solstices, creating a unique experience for viewers as they observe the changing light and shadows throughout the day. "Sun Tunnels" is considered a significant example of Land Art, as it integrates natural elements with human-made structures to create a harmonious relationship between art and the environment.
- The work is intended to be experienced over time, as visitors can stay overnight in a nearby cabin to witness the changing light and weather conditions that affect the appearance of the tunnels. "Sun Tunnels" challenges traditional notions of art by emphasizing the relationship between humans and nature, and it has become an iconic example of Land Art in the United States.
