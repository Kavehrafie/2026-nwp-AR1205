## Art Movements of the 1960s and 1970s
#### WEEK 14


:: note ::

April 8, 2026


<!--

### Pop Art ✏️

<!-- 
Pop Art emerged in the late 1950s as a reaction against Abstract Expressionism's inward focus, embracing the imagery and techniques of mass culture with a playful, ironic detachment. Artists like Andy Warhol, Roy Lichtenstein, and Claes Oldenburg transformed everyday objects and media into art, blurring the boundaries between high and low culture. The movement reflected and critiqued the consumer culture explosion of postwar America, where television, advertising, and celebrity became dominant forces. Pop Art's use of commercial aesthetics and mass-produced imagery challenged traditional notions of artistic originality and authenticity, while also commenting on the commodification of art itself.

- Consumer culture explosion: 87% of American homes have a TV by 1960; the average person encounters roughly 1,600 ads per day
- Art market boom: Pop Art prices escalated 4,000% by the end of the 1960s — thirty galleries in New York in 1950 became three hundred by 1960

## Minimalism ✏️

<!-- 

Minimalism emerged in the 1960s as a reaction against the emotional intensity and subjective expression of Abstract Expressionism, embracing simplicity, industrial materials, and geometric forms to create art that was self-referential and focused on the physical experience of space. Artists like Donald Judd, Dan Flavin, and Carl Andre rejected traditional notions of composition and narrative, instead emphasizing the objecthood of the artwork and its relationship to the viewer. Minimalist works often featured serial repetition, modularity, and a lack of ornamentation, challenging viewers to engage with the materiality and spatial presence of the piece rather than seeking symbolic meaning. The movement reflected broader cultural shifts towards consumerism, technology, and a desire for clarity in an increasingly complex world.

-->

---

![Donald Judd, Untitled, 1969. Copper, Ten units, each 9 x 40 x 31 inches (22.9 x 101.6 x 78.7 cm), with 9-inch (22.9 cm) intervals; overall height: 15 feet (457.2 cm)](https://res.cloudinary.com/image-solar/image/upload/v1775838916/2026nwp-ar1205/91.3713_ph_web-1_g5vanu.jpg)

<!--
Donald Judd’s *Untitled* (1969) is a vertical stack of ten identical copper boxes with translucent Plexiglas tops, bolted to the gallery wall at precisely equal intervals — a work that is neither sculpture nor relief but what Judd called a “specific object,” insisting that art be grasped as a whole rather than parsed as a composition of parts, and that space itself is the medium.

**Visual and formal analysis**
- Ten identical copper boxes, each 9 × 40 × 31 inches, mounted to the wall with 9-inch intervals between them; serial repetition replaces compositional choice with mathematical system
- Translucent Plexiglas tops reveal that the boxes are hollow — they are not solid masses but containers of space, making void as materially important as volume
- The highly polished copper surface reflects the surrounding gallery, collapsing the boundary between the object and its environment; color and light shift as the viewer moves
- Judd specified that the units should extend from floor to ceiling, making the room’s architecture part of the work — the piece cannot exist independently of its site

**Historical context**
- Judd abandoned painting in the early 1960s, declaring that “actual space is intrinsically more powerful and specific than paint on a flat surface”
- His 1965 essay “Specific Objects” championed work that was neither painting nor sculpture, endorsing “the thing as a whole” — Lee Bontecou, Oldenburg, and Stella were his reference points
- The boxes were fabricated industrially, not made by Judd’s own hands — an ethical and aesthetic stance: the artist as designer, not craftsman, embracing the materials of industrial culture
- Doss frames Minimalism as emerging alongside JFK’s New Frontier, the Civil Rights movement, and Vietnam War escalation — Frank Stella’s dictum “what you see is what you see” rejected both AbEx subjectivity and Pop irony in favor of literal, industrial presence
- Artforum became the movement’s theoretical platform; structuralist and information theory shaped the critical discourse around seriality and systems

**Iconographic significance**
- Serial repetition refuses narrative, hierarchy, and focal point — every unit is identical, echoing industrial mass production and a kind of democratic indifference
- The wall-mounted stack eliminates the pedestal, dissolving the boundary between “art object” and “architectural environment” — the gallery itself becomes part of the work
- Judd’s mathematical progressions (in other works, explicit Fibonacci sequences) substitute system for subjective composition — the artist’s hand disappears into a predetermined rule
- Doss raises the key question Minimalism forced: if the artist didn’t make it with his own hands, who is the artist? The movement provoked a reckoning with authorship, labor, and the definition of art itself
-->

### Land Art ✏️
</div>

<!--
Nancy Holt’s *Sun Tunnels* (1973–76) consists of four massive concrete cylinders arranged in an open X configuration in Utah’s Great Basin Desert, aligned to frame the sunrise and sunset at the summer and winter solstices—a work that sculpts sunlight through the interplay of land, sky, and astronomical time, and that must be traveled to, waited in, and experienced over hours and days.

**Visual and formal analysis**
- Four concrete cylinders, each 18 feet long and 9 feet in diameter, are positioned in an X pattern across the desert floor, creating framed views of the vast landscape
- Drilled holes in each tunnel cast constellation-shaped patterns of light — Capricorn, Columba, Draco, and Perseus — that shift continuously as the sun moves, making light itself the medium
- The work’s appearance transforms with weather, season, and time of day—duration is embedded as a structural element of the piece

**Historical context**
- Created during the Land Art movement of the 1970s, alongside Robert Smithson’s *Spiral Jetty* and Walter De Maria’s *Lightning Field*
- Acquired and stewarded by the Dia Art Foundation; reflects the period’s impulse to move art out of commercial galleries and into the landscape
- Holt’s focus on perception, celestial observation, and embodied experience distinguished her from the more masculinist rhetoric of her Land Art peers
- Doss notes that these Minimalist artforms tapped "universal time" and ancient mythologies — a precedent for Feminist art’s simultaneous interest in universal and archetypal forms, though both raise the question: whose universals?

**Iconographic significance**
- Frames the sun as both subject and medium, transforming the viewer from a spectator into a participant who must travel to and dwell within the work
- The solstice alignment connects the installation to ancient astronomical observatories like Stonehenge, linking contemporary art to prehistoric monument-making traditions
- Challenges the commodification of art: immovable, experiential, and dependent on natural phenomena, *Sun Tunnels* resists gallery ownership and photographic reproduction
- Doss observes that Holt’s constellation-drilled tunnels coupled "ancient mythologies with science fiction" — the work looks backward to prehistoric monument-making and forward to an art freed from the market entirely

## From Conceptual Art to Feminist Art ✏️

<!-- 

Conceptual Art emerged in the 1960s as a movement that prioritized ideas and concepts over traditional aesthetic and material concerns, challenging the notion of art as a physical object. Artists like Sol LeWitt, Joseph Kosuth, and Yoko Ono created works that were often ephemeral, text-based, or performance-oriented, emphasizing the intellectual engagement of the viewer rather than visual pleasure. Conceptual Art questioned the commodification of art and the role of the artist, proposing that the idea behind a work could be more important than its execution. This movement laid the groundwork for Feminist Art, which emerged in the late 1960s and early 1970s as a response to the male-dominated art world, seeking to challenge gender stereotypes and reclaim women's experiences and bodies as valid subjects of artistic expression.

-->

---

![Joseph Kosuth, _One and Three Chairs_, 1965](https://www.moma.org/media/W1siZiIsIjE3MDMzMSJdLFsicCIsImNvbnZlcnQiLCItcXVhbGl0eSA5MCAtcmVzaXplIDIwMDB4MjAwMFx1MDAzZSJdXQ.jpg?sha=9ec6b1b0f44ccf97)

<!-- 
One of artists associated with Art Workers Coalition \

In One and Three Chairs, Joseph Kosuth represents one chair three ways: as a manufactured chair, as a photograph, and as a copy of a dictionary entry for the word “chair.” The installation is thus composed of an object, an image, and words.

Kosuth didn’t make the chair, take the photograph, or write the definition; he selected and assembled them together. But is this art? And which representation of the chair is most “accurate”? These open-ended questions are exactly what Kosuth wanted us to think about when he said that “art is making meaning.” By assembling these three alternative representations, Kosuth turns a simple wooden chair into an object of debate and even consternation, a platform for exploring new meanings.

Additional text
A chair sits alongside a photograph of a chair and a dictionary definition of the word chair. Perhaps all three are chairs, or codes for one: a visual code, a verbal code, and a code in the language of objects, that is, a chair of wood. But isn't this last chair simply . . . a chair? Or, as Marcel Duchamp asked in his Bicycle Wheel of 1913, does the inclusion of an object in an artwork somehow change it? If both photograph and words describe a chair, how is their functioning different from that of the real chair, and what is Kosuth's artwork doing by adding these functions together? Prodded to ask such questions, the viewer embarks on the basic processes demanded by Conceptual art.

"The art I call conceptual is such because it is based on an inquiry into the nature of art," Kosuth has written. "Thus, it is . . . a working out, a thinking out, of all the implications of all aspects of the concept 'art,' . . . Fundamental to this idea of art is the understanding of the linguistic nature of all art propositions, be they past or present, and regardless of the elements used in their construction." Chasing a chair through three different registers, Kosuth asks us to try to decipher the subliminal sentences in which we phrase our experience of art.


-->

<!--

**Visual and formal analysis**
- The body print technique — margarine on skin, pressed to paper, dusted with pigment — produces a ghostly, X-ray-like image that is simultaneously indexical (a literal trace of the artist's body) and spectral (the figure appears as a bruise or afterimage)
- The bound figure, gagged and tethered, is enclosed by a US flag that functions as both frame and cage — the national symbol doubles as instrument of repression
- Monochrome palette (dark pigment on off-white paper) strips the image to its essentials: body, binding, flag — no ornament, no distraction

- Made in 1970, one year after the Chicago Eight trial, in which Bobby Seale was bound, gagged, and chained to his chair by order of Judge Julius Hoffman — an image that circulated widely and became a rallying point for the Black Power movement

- Doss places Hammons within a Black Art movement that ranged from militant protest (Faith Ringgold's bleeding flags) to ironic deconstruction (Robert Colescott's recast history paintings) to abstract sculpture (Melvin Edwards's welded *Lynch Fragments*)
- The work also exists in tension with the largely white Feminist Art movement: Black women artists like Betye Saar and Faith Ringgold were caught between Feminism and Black Power, navigating both movements' blind spots around race and gender

- The body print makes the artist's own flesh into evidence — a forensic, almost legalistic strategy that mirrors the courtroom setting it depicts
- The flag as frame collapses the distance between symbol and reality: the nation that promises liberty is literally the container that binds and silences
- Doss notes that Hammons's body prints "presaged an ongoing exploration of black subjectivity" — his later works with hair, chicken bones, and basketball hoops extended this fusion of material culture with racial identity
